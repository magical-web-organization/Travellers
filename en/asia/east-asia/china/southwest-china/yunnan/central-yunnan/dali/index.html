Introduction to Dali

Located in Central Yunnan in Southwest China, Dali is a charming ancient city that dates back to the Ming Dynasty. The city is surrounded by the stunning Cangshan Mountains and the serene Erhai Lake, making it an ideal destination for outdoor activities and scenic views.

In this comprehensive travel guide for Dali, we will introduce you to the must-see attractions, hiking and biking routes, where to stay, what to eat and drink, and much more.

List of Places to Visit in Dali

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  1. Three Pagodas: The iconic Three Pagodas are located in the Chongsheng Temple Complex and are a symbol of the ancient architecture of the Dali Kingdom.
  2. Dali Ancient City: This walled city is home to over 1,300 years of history and culture. Explore the narrow streets, shops, and restaurants, and witness traditional architecture exemplified by the Bai ethnic minority.
  3. Butterfly Spring Park: This park is famous for its butterfly-shaped fountain, which changes color according to the season. There are also several temples and pagodas on its landscaped grounds.
  4. Dali Museum: A modern museum that showcases the rich history and culture of the Bai people through a range of exhibits, interactive displays, and movie screenings.

Natural Attractions of Dali: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  1. Erhai Lake: The second-largest freshwater lake in Yunnan, Erhai Lake is surrounded by mountains and offers stunning views and a relaxing atmosphere. Activities such as fishing and boating tours are available in the lake region.
  2. Cangshan Mountain Range: The Cangshan Mountain Range provides a breathtaking backdrop to the city of Dali. There are over 20 peaks, each with unique views and hiking opportunities.
  3. Gantong Cable Car: Gantong Cable Car provides spectacular views of the Cangshan Mountains and Erhai Lake. It goes up to the Yunfeng Temple at 2,400 meters above sea level.
  4. Shuanglang Ancient Town: Located by the Erhai Lake, Shuanglang Ancient Town is a classic Bai village. Visitors can rent bicycles to explore the old town or take a boat ride on the lake.
  5. Xizhou Bai Minority Village: This traditional village is famous for its Bai architecture and unique handcrafts made by the local artisans, including tie-dye, handmade paper-making, and silverwork.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Dali

  1. Golden Shuttle Route: A scenic hiking trail around Cangshan Mountain, featuring panoramic views of the lake and the mountains. A cable car can take you up and down the mountain.
  2. Dali Greenways: A network of bike trails throughout Dali, newly developed and featuring lovely views of the surrounding scenery.

Best Things to Do in Dali: Activities and Tours

  1. Bai Ethnic Minority Cultural Show: A performance showcasing Bai culture and traditions, including music, dance, and costumes.
  2. Chinese Calligraphy Class: Learn the ancient art of Chinese calligraphy and create your own Chinese artwork to take home.
  3. Traditional Chinese Tea Ceremony: Participate in a traditional tea ceremony and learn about the history of tea in China.
  4. Cooking Class: Learn to prepare authentic local dishes in a traditional Bai cooking class.

Week-Long Itinerary for Dali

Here is a suggested itinerary for a week-long trip to Dali:

Day Activity
1 Explore Dali old town, visit the Three Pagodas and Chongsheng Temple complex
2 Take the Gantong Cable Car to Yunfeng Temple and go hiking in the Cangshan Mountains
3 Go bicycling around Erhai Lake and explore Shuanglang Ancient Town
4 Take a Chinese calligraphy class in Dali Museum and visit Xizhou Bai Minority Village
5 Attend the Bai Ethnic Minority Cultural Show and take a traditional tea ceremony class
6 Join a cooking class and learn to prepare local dishes or spend a day of leisure by the tranquil lake
7 Depart Dali or head to nearby Lijiang or Shangri-La for further adventures.

Food and Drink Scene of Dali

Must-Try Dishes

  1. Xizhou Baba: A favorite meal among locals, Xizhou Baba is a crispy pancake with meat or vegetable stuffing
  2. Rushan (Melting Cheese): A traditional meets western fusion, Rushan is fried cheese served with tea or fruits.
  3. Erkuai: similar to rice noodles, Erkuai is steamed and then fried with various combinations of meat, vegetables and seasonings.
  4. Yunnan Hotpot: Yunnan Hotpot is a spicy hotpot made with fresh vegetables, mushrooms, meats, and seafood. Dip them in a spicy sauce for added flavor.

Local Beverages

  1. Dali Beer: A locally produced beer, Dali Beer is a refreshing brew that complements spicy dishes.
  2. Plum Wine: A speciality of Dali, Plum Wine is a local fruit wine unique to the region.
  3. Yunnan Tea: Yunnan is known for its tea production. Try the full-bodied Pu’er Tea, known for its health benefits.

Where to Stay in Dali: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

  1. Dali Old Town Hostels: Hostels located in Dali’s ancient town offer both budget-friendly and private rooms. Some include breakfast and bike rental services.
  2. Linden Centre: A historic guesthouse providing guests with a chance to stay in a traditional Bai courtyard with a modern twist, while indulging in local specialties like traditional Bai amuse-bouche.
  3. Empark Grand Hotel Dali: A five-star luxury hotel located in a serene spot of Dali, offering guest access to scenic views, comfort, and luxe amenities.

What to Buy in Dali: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

  1. Tie-Dye Textiles: Known as the hometown of tie-dye, Dali is famed for its tie-dye textiles. Be sure to take some back home, as a souvenir or a gift for your loved ones.
  2. Handcrafted Paper: Handmade paper in Xizhou is exported to other parts of China and is regarded as a precious material for calligraphy or painting artworks.
  3. Tea Leaves: Yunnan is famous for its tea production, and Dali is no exception. Dali is home to many tea shops selling tea, including the famous Pu’er tea.

Local Culture and Customs of Dali

Dali, known for its Bai ethnic culture and traditions, has remained well-preserved to this day. Bai people are hospitable and generous; it’s common courtesy to return their hospitality by showing gratitude, respect and politeness. Locals observe traditional holidays, including the Lantern Festival and the Bai New Year.

Best Time to Visit Dali

The ideal time to visit Dali is from September to November, when the weather is mild, the skies are clear, and the autumn foliage is at its peak. Travelers are advised to avoid visiting Dali during the peak summer season in July and August, which is often hot and humid.

Do’s and Don’ts of Dali

Do:

  1. Take your shoes off while entering a local household, temples or other public indoor space.
  2. Respect the local culture, traditions and customs.
  3. Try to learn a few basic phrases of the Bai language, and don’t forget to say “ni hao” (hello).

Don’t:

  1. Don’t use your feet to point at someone or things, or step over someone’s feet.
  2. Don’t touch people’s heads, and don’t pat children or infants on the head.
  3. Don’t discuss politics or religion; public discussions on these topics are not typically welcomed.

Safety and Health

Safety in Dali

Dali is generally a safe city with low levels of crime. Petty theft, like pickpocketing, can occur in crowded areas. Travelers are advised to keep their belongings secure and stay aware of their surroundings.

Health in Dali

Altitude sickness can be a concern for those who travel up the mountain in Dali. It’s essential to drink plenty of water and take breaks when hiking or biking. Health facilities in Dali might be limited or lack availability of English-speaking medical professionals, so always carry international health insurance and a translated document stating medical information in English and Chinese.

Getting to Dali: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Dali

Dali is serviced by Dali Airport, located approximately 12 km from Dali old town. Flights from major cities in China are available, including Kunming, Chengdu, and Beijing.

Bus Stations Near Dali

There are four bus stations in Dali:

  1. Dali Express Bus Station: Located at the south entrance of Dali old town, this station mainly services long-distance buses to Lijiang, Kunming, Shaxi, and other destinations.
  2. Dali North Bus Station: This station mainly services minibuses to Shuanglang, Erhai Lake, and other nearby areas.
  3. Dali South Bus Station: How to Dali airport and other nearby towns, such as Yangbi, Tincheng, Weishan, and more.
  4. Dali West Bus Station: This station provides buses to Xiaguan and other parts of Yunnan Province.

Other Options

Taxis, ride-hailing services, and rental cars are readily available in Dali.

How to Get Around in Dali

Dali city and surrounding areas are most convenient to travel on foot, bicycle, or taxi. Minibuses and boats can be rented for transportation in the countryside or on Erhai Lake.

Where to Go Next from Dali: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

  1. Lijiang: A UNESCO World Heritage Site renowned for its ancient town, cobbled stone pathways, stunning nature and traditional culture.
  2. Shaxi: A classically preserved ancient market town located in the mountains of Yunnan, often referred to as a small Lijiang.
  3. Shangri-La: A mystical, charming city located in the northernmost part of Yunnan, bordering with Tibet Autonomous Region, Home to splendid mountain scenery as well as rich Tibetan and ethnic culture

Conclusion

Dali is a gem of Southwest China with rich history, unique culture, and stunning natural beauty. Take the opportunity to try local food, admire ancient architecture, explore the mountain range, and learn about the Bai ethnic culture. Safe journey and have fun exploring Dali!
