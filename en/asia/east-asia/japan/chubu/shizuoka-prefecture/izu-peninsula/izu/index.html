Introduction to Izu

Izu is a scenic peninsula located in Shizuoka Prefecture, Japan that is popular among tourists for its natural hot springs, stunning coastal landscapes, historic landmarks, and cultural attractions. It is situated in the Chubu region of Japan, which is also known as the Japanese Alps. The peninsula is home to several quaint towns and villages, such as Atami, Ito, and Shimoda. Whether you are seeking adventurous outdoor activities, cultural experiences, or relaxation in a tranquil environment, Izu has something for everyone.

List of Places to Visit in Izu

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

Izu has a rich cultural heritage that can be experienced through its many museums and historical sites. Some of the must-visit attractions in this category include:

  • Izu Shaboten Zoo: This zoo is famous for its impressive collection of cacti and other succulent plants, as well as its wide variety of animals such as tigers, lions, and alpacas.

  • Kadowaki Suspension Bridge: This 48-meter-long suspension bridge offers stunning views of the surrounding mountains, forests, and waterfalls.

  • Nirayama Reverberatory Furnace: This historic site was once used to produce iron during the Edo period and is now a UNESCO World Heritage site.

Natural Attractions of Izu: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

Izu is renowned for its pristine natural beauty that includes mountains, forests, beaches, and hot springs. Some of the top natural attractions that you must visit in Izu are:

  • Mount Omuro: This volcano is known for its unique circular shape and offers spectacular views of the surrounding landscapes. Visitors can also enjoy a thrilling ride on the Omuro Chairlift to the summit.

  • Jogasaki Coast: This picturesque coastal region boasts stunning rock formations, cliffs, and panoramic views of the Pacific Ocean.

  • Ohito River Valley: This valley is an ideal destination for hikers and nature enthusiasts, featuring several scenic trails that run through lush forests, waterfalls, and traditional villages.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Izu

Izu has many hiking and biking trails that allow visitors to explore its natural wonders up close. Some of the best routes include:

  • Mount Daruma Hike: This 6-kilometer round-trip trail takes hikers to the summit of Mount Daruma, which offers stunning panoramic views of the Izu Peninsula.

  • Kanaya to Jogasaki Coast Bike Route: This 18-kilometer bike route takes visitors through scenic coastal roads and a scenic route that runs along several beaches and rocky cliffs.

Best Things to Do in Izu: Activities and Tours

Apart from the natural and cultural attractions, Izu has plenty of activities and tours that cater to a wide range of interests. Some of the top things to do in Izu are:

  • Onsen Hopping: Izu is famous for its natural hot springs, and visitors can experience a variety of onsen, such as Hakone Yumoto Onsen, Atami Onsen, and Shuzenji Onsen.

  • Sea Kayaking: The coastal waters of Izu are ideal for sea kayaking, which allows visitors to explore the rocky coastline and enjoy stunning views of the Pacific Ocean.

  • Wine Tasting: Izu is known for its wineries, such as Atami Meissen, and visitors can enjoy wine tasting tours that include sampling some of the best wines of the region.

Week-Long Itinerary for Izu

For visitors who wish to spend a week exploring the wonders of Izu, here is an ideal itinerary:

Day 1 Explore Atami, visit Atami Castle and Atami Onsen
Day 2 Visit Mount Omuro, go on Omuro Chairlift

Food and Drink Scene of Izu

Must-Try Dishes

Izu has a rich culinary heritage that features fresh seafood, traditional Japanese cuisine, and locally grown produce. Some of the top dishes to try in Izu are:

  • Sashimi: Fresh, raw seafood from the Pacific Ocean is a specialty of Izu, with recommended dishes such as maguro (tuna), ika (squid) and tako (octopus)

  • Wasabi: Izu is known for producing some of the best wasabi in Japan, which is often paired with sushi, sashimi, and other local dishes.

  • Sakura Shrimp: These tiny, pinkish shrimp are caught in the waters off Izu and are renowned for their delicate flavor and texture.

Local Beverages

Izu is famous for its sake, which is made from locally grown rice and pure freshwater. Some of the best breweries to visit for tasting are:

  • Imayotsukasa Sake Brewery: This brewery produces a range of sake varieties that are made using traditional brewing methods.

  • Olasake Brewery: This artisanal brewery makes sake in small batches, using only locally sourced ingredients.

Where to Stay in Izu: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Izu offers a range of accommodation options that cater to different budgets and preferences. Some of the popular places to stay in Izu include:

  • Izukogen-no-Yado Konya: This traditional Japanese-style ryokan offers spacious tatami rooms, onsen, and a restaurant that serves local cuisine.

  • Bella Vista Spa and Marina Onsen Hotel: This upscale hotel features scenic views of Sagami Bay, luxurious suites with private onsens, and a range of dining options.

  • K’s Hostel Ito Onsen: This budget-friendly hostel offers dormitory-style rooms, private rooms, and a communal kitchen. It is conveniently located near Ito Onsen Station.

What to Buy in Izu: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

Izu is famous for its traditional crafts, such as pottery, woodwork, and washi paper. Visitors can purchase a range of souvenirs and local products at the following places:

  • Izu Kokeshi Museum: This museum features a collection of traditional kokeshi dolls, which are a popular souvenir from Izu.

  • Shuzenji Niji-no-Sato: This shopping complex offers a variety of locally produced goods, such as wasabi products, tea, ceramics, and other crafts.

Local Culture and Customs of Izu

Izu has a unique culture that reflects its history and geography. The locals are known for their warm hospitality and dedication to preserving their traditions. Some of the cultural customs to be aware of when visiting Izu include:

  • Respecting Personal Space: Japanese culture places a great emphasis on personal space, and visitors are advised to be mindful of their surroundings and avoid unnecessary physical contact when interacting with the locals.

  • Observing Onsen Etiquette: Onsen bathing is a traditional Japanese custom, and visitors are expected to adhere to certain etiquette, such as cleansing themselves before entering the baths, covering their private parts, and refraining from using towels inside the bath.

Best Time to Visit Izu

Izu can be visited throughout the year, but the best time to visit depends on your interests and preferences. Spring (March-May) and fall (September-November) are the ideal seasons to visit for mild weather that’s perfect for outdoor activities. Summer (June-August) is the peak tourist season in Izu, and it’s the ideal season for beach activities. Winter (December-February) is the off-season, but it’s a great time to enjoy the hot springs and explore the less crowded attractions.

Do’s and Don’ts of Izu

Do:

  • Learn some basic Japanese phrases: Visitors are advised to learn some basic phrases such as “arigato” (thank you), “sumimasen” (excuse me), and “konnichiwa” (hello) to help them interact with the locals.

  • Try local cuisine: Izu has a diverse culinary landscape, and visitors are encouraged to try local foods and beverages, such as wasabi, sashimi, and sake.

Don’t:

  • Wear shoes inside traditional houses: In Japan, visitors are expected to remove their shoes before entering traditional houses, temples, and other public buildings.

  • Litter or smoke in public: Japan has strict laws against littering and smoking in public places, and visitors are advised to dispose of their trash responsibly and avoid smoking in public areas.

Safety and Health

Safety in Izu

Izu is a safe destination, but visitors are advised to take basic safety precautions, such as securing their valuables, staying vigilant in crowded areas, and obeying traffic rules. In case of emergencies, visitors can seek help from the police or call the emergency hotline number 110.

Health in Izu

Izu is known for its natural hot springs, which are believed to have therapeutic benefits for several health conditions. Visitors who are planning to visit the hot springs are advised to consult their doctor first, especially if they have any pre-existing medical conditions. Visitors are also advised to stay hydrated, use sun protection, and avoid overexertion during outdoor activities.

Getting to Izu: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Izu

The nearest airport to Izu is Tokyo Haneda International Airport, which is located approximately 140 km away. Visitors can take a direct bus or train from the airport to Izu.

Bus Stations Near Izu

Izu has several bus stations that connect it to other parts of Japan, such as Tokyo, Yokohama, and Osaka. Some of the popular bus stations in Izu include Ito Station and Shuzenji Station.

Other Options

Visitors can also travel to Izu by train, which is a popular and convenient option. The Tokaido Shinkansen and JR Ito Line both offer services to Izu. Visitors can also rent a car and drive to Izu, which allows them to explore the peninsula at their own pace.

How to Get Around in Izu

Izu has an extensive and efficient transportation network that includes trains, buses, taxis, and rental cars. Trains are the most popular method of transportation in Izu, with the JR Ito Line and Izukyu Railway offering services that connect major tourist destinations. Visitors can also take the local buses, which cover the less accessible parts of Izu.

Where to Go Next from Izu: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

Izu is located near several popular tourist destinations in Japan, including:

  • Tokyo: This vibrant capital city is only a few hours away from Izu and offers a range of attractions such as the Tokyo Skytree, Sensoji Temple, and Akihabara.

  • Hakone: This picturesque region is famous for its onsen, scenic hiking trails, and Lake Ashinoko.

  • Kyoto: The ancient city of Kyoto is famous for its historic temples and shrines, including Kinkakuji, Fushimi Inari Shrine, and Kiyomizudera.

Conclusion

Izu is a hidden gem of Japan that offers a perfect blend of natural beauty, adventure, culture, and relaxation. We hope our comprehensive guide has helped you plan your trip and explore the best of this picturesque peninsula. Whether you are visiting Izu for a day or a week, we guarantee that you will fall in love with its unique charm and hospitality.
