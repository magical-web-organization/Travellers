Introduction to Wadi Rum

Wadi Rum is a stunning desert located in southern Jordan, in the heart of the Middle East. Boasting surreal rock formations, endless sand dunes, and a truly unique landscape, it’s no wonder that Wadi Rum is often referred to as the “Valley of the Moon”. With a rich history dating back thousands of years, Wadi Rum is also steeped in culture and heritage, meaning that it has something to offer travelers of all kinds.

List of Places to Visit in Wadi Rum

Whether you’re interested in exploring Wadi Rum’s historically significant attractions or you’re more intrigued by the outdoor adventures and scenic vistas to be found here, there’s something for everyone in this spectacular desert. Here’s a closer look at some of the must-see places in Wadi Rum.

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

Wadi Rum is a land of ancient history and culture, featuring many museums and historical sites that are worth exploring. For instance, the Wadi Rum visitors’ center offers a wealth of information on the desert’s history, flora and fauna, as well as its Bedouin inhabitants. You can also visit the site where T. E. Lawrence, better known as Lawrence of Arabia, had his desert fortress during World War I.

Natural Attractions of Wadi Rum: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

For those who love outdoor adventures and stunning natural scenery, Wadi Rum is the perfect destination. Some of the most popular things to do here include hiking or biking around the dunes, jeep tours of the desert, and camel rides through the sand. You’ll also want to be sure to stop at some of the best viewpoints for can’t-miss views of the surrounding area, such as the famous Red Sand Dune.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Wadi Rum

If you’re looking for the best hiking and biking routes in Wadi Rum, then you’re in luck. The desert offers a variety of trails to suit all skill levels and interests.

Best Things to Do in Wadi Rum: Activities and Tours

Beyond hiking, biking, and sightseeing, there are many other things to see and do in Wadi Rum that will give you a better sense of the desert’s unique history and culture. For instance, you might consider signing up for a sunset or stargazing tour, or spending time with local Bedouin tribes to learn more about their ways of life and traditions.

Week-Long Itinerary for Wadi Rum

If you’re planning to spend a week in Wadi Rum, then you’ll want to make sure you have time to see everything that the desert has to offer. Here’s a sample itinerary that will give you a good idea of how to make the most of your time in Wadi Rum:

Day 1: Arrive in Wadi Rum, and spend the afternoon exploring the visitors center and learning about the area’s history and culture. Day 2: Take an early-morning camel ride, then spend the day hiking through the dunes. Day 3: Rent a mountain bike, and tackle one of Wadi Rum’s many scenic bike paths. Day 4: Go on a jeep tour to explore some of the desert’s more remote and rugged areas. Day 5: Explore Wadi Rum’s iconic rock formations on foot, then take a sunset tour to see the colors of the desert come alive. Day 6: Spend the day with a local Bedouin tribe, learning about their heritage and way of life. Day 7: Depart Wadi Rum.

Food and Drink Scene of Wadi Rum

No trip to Wadi Rum would be complete without sampling some of the desert’s famous food and drink. Here are a few of the must-try dishes and local beverages that you won’t want to miss:

Must-Try Dishes

  • Mansaf: This traditional Bedouin dish is made with lamb or beef, rice, and yogurt sauce, and is often served at festive occasions.
  • Zarb: A unique Bedouin-style barbeque, where meat and vegetables are cooked underground for several hours.
  • Makloubeh: A hearty one-pot meal made with chicken, eggplant, and rice.

Local Beverages

  • Bedouin tea: Made with sage and other herbs, Bedouin tea is a staple of life in Wadi Rum.
  • Arabic coffee: Also known as “Turkish coffee”, this strong, sweet coffee is brewed with cardamom and is perfect for sipping with a view of the desert.

Where to Stay in Wadi Rum: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Wadi Rum has no shortage of accommodation options, ranging from budget-friendly hostels to luxurious desert camps. No matter your budget or travel style, there’s a lodging option that’s perfect for you.

What to Buy in Wadi Rum: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

If you’re in the market for souvenirs and gifts, there are plenty of unique items to be found in Wadi Rum. Some of the most popular local products include traditional Bedouin handicrafts, such as woven baskets and textiles, as well as spices, herbs, and oils from the local markets.

Local Culture and Customs of Wadi Rum

To truly appreciate all that Wadi Rum has to offer, it’s important to understand some of the local customs and cultural practices. For instance, visitors should dress conservatively and be mindful of local dress codes, especially when visiting religious or historical sites. Visitors should also be aware that alcohol is not widely available in Wadi Rum due to Islamic laws.

Best Time to Visit Wadi Rum

The best time to visit Wadi Rum is in the shoulder seasons, during spring and fall, when temperatures are mild and crowds are smaller. Summer can be quite hot, so visitors should be prepared for high temperatures if they visit during the peak summer months.

Do’s and Don’ts of Wadi Rum

To ensure a safe and enjoyable trip to Wadi Rum, visitors should keep the following do’s and don’ts in mind:

Do:

  • Dress conservatively and respect local customs and traditions.
  • Drink plenty of water and stay hydrated, especially during summer.
  • Respect the delicate desert environment and avoid littering or damaging natural features.

Don’t:

  • Drink alcohol or bring it with you, as it is not widely accepted in Wadi Rum.
  • Wander off on your own, especially at night or in remote areas.
  • Engage in activities that may harm the desert environment, such as off-roading or rock-climbing without proper permits and training.

Safety and Health

While Wadi Rum is a relatively safe destination, visitors should still take precautions to ensure their safety and health.

Safety in Wadi Rum

Travelers should be aware of any potential dangers when exploring the desert or engaging in outdoor activities. It’s important to always follow the advice of your guide, stick to designated trails, and avoid wandering off on your own. Visitors should also be cautious when driving on the desert roads, as they can be narrow and winding.

Health in Wadi Rum

Visitors to Wadi Rum should be sure to keep up to date with their vaccinations and take precautions against mosquito bites, which can transmit diseases such as Malaria and Dengue fever. It’s also important to stay hydrated and avoid excessive exposure to the sun, as the desert climate can be very harsh.

Getting to Wadi Rum: Transportation Options and Logistics

Wadi Rum is easily accessible from major cities and transportation hubs like Amman, Petra, and Aqaba.

Airports Near Wadi Rum

The nearest airport to Wadi Rum is King Hussein International Airport, which is located in Aqaba. From there, visitors can arrange car or taxi transfers to Wadi Rum, which typically take around 40 minutes.

Bus Stations Near Wadi Rum

Several different bus companies operate routes to Wadi Rum, including JETT Bus and Al-Ghanem Bus. Buses typically depart from major cities such as Amman or Petra and can be a budget-friendly option for travelers on a budget.

Other Options

Private transfers or car rentals can also be arranged to get to and from Wadi Rum, and visitors can even participate in self-guided driving tours of the desert.

How to Get Around in Wadi Rum

Once you arrive in Wadi Rum, the best way to get around is by taking a guided tour or hiring a local Bedouin guide. There are also hiking and biking trails that visitors can explore on their own, as well as jeep rentals for those who prefer a more rugged off-road experience.

Where to Go Next from Wadi Rum: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

Wadi Rum is located in close proximity to several other popular destinations in Jordan, including Petra, the Dead Sea, and Aqaba. Visitors can easily arrange day trips or multi-day tours to explore these other areas of the country and experience different facets of Jordanian culture and history.

Conclusion

A visit to Wadi Rum is a must for anyone traveling to Jordan or looking for a unique desert adventure. With a rich history and culture to explore, stunning natural scenery to admire, and a wide range of activities and tours to enjoy, there’s something for everyone in this otherworldly landscape. By following the tips and recommendations outlined in this guide, you’ll be well-equipped to plan the trip of a lifetime to Wadi Rum.
