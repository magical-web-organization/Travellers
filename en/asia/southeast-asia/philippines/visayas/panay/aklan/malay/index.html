Introduction to Malay

Malay is a small town located in the province of Aklan on the island of Panay in the Visayas region of the Philippines. This quiet and laid-back town is often overlooked by tourists but offers a unique glimpse into the local culture and natural beauty of the Philippines. Malay is also the gateway to Boracay Island, one of the most popular tourist destinations in the Philippines.

In this comprehensive travel guide, we will share everything you need to know to plan your trip to Malay and make the most of your time in this charming town.

List of Places to Visit in Malay

Malay has a variety of attractions to offer visitors, from historic landmarks to natural wonders. Here are some of the must-see places in and around Malay:

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • Museo it Akean: This museum showcases the rich cultural heritage of the Aklanon people through exhibits of traditional clothing, tools, and artifacts.
  • Bakhawan Eco-Park and Research Centre: A 220-hectare mangrove forest which is home to numerous bird species, crabs, and other marine creatures.
  • Ibajay Ancestral House: This historic house features antique furniture and artifacts that give visitors a glimpse into life in the area during the 19th century.

Natural Attractions of Malay: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • Mount Madjaas: This mountain offers an exciting hiking experience for experienced climbers, with breathtaking views from the summit.
  • Nabaoy River: A beautiful river surrounded by lush greenery and popular for river tubing and swimming.
  • White Beach: This stunning white sand beach is perfect for swimming, sunbathing, and water activities like snorkeling and diving.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Malay

Malay boasts some of the best hiking and biking routes in the Philippines, with trails ranging in difficulty from easy to challenging. Some popular routes include:

  • Tuburan Trail: A moderately difficult hike that takes about three hours, with beautiful views of the mountains and valleys.
  • Oyang Landing to Tinago Water Falls: A scenic hike that passes through rice paddies and forests before reaching the stunning Tinago Waterfalls.
  • Mount Luho Trail: A challenging hike that takes around three hours to reach the summit, with panoramic views of the island and ocean.

Best Things to Do in Malay: Activities and Tours

Aside from hiking and biking, there are many other activities and tours to enjoy in Malay:

  • Island Hopping: Explore the surrounding islands of Malay, including Carabao, Pan de Azucar, and Crocodile Island.
  • Scuba Diving: Discover the underwater world of Malay, with diving spots like Yapak, Crocodile Island, and Manoc-Manoc.
  • Parasailing: Take a thrilling ride above the crystal-clear waters of Malay and get a bird’s-eye view of the island.

Food and Drink Scene of Malay

Malay has a diverse food scene that blends local flavors with international cuisine. Here are some must-try dishes and local beverages:

Must-try Dishes

  • Chicken Inasal: Grilled chicken marinated in local spices and served with rice and a soy-vinegar dipping sauce.
  • Kinilaw: A traditional Filipino ceviche made with fresh fish, vinegar, and spices.
  • Lechon Kawali: Deep-fried pork belly, crispy and succulent.

Local Beverages

  • Boracay Island Brewery: A microbrewery that serves locally brewed beer and other refreshing beverages.
  • Calamansi Juice: A refreshing drink made from locally grown calamansi fruit.
  • Tubâ: A sweet and tangy beverage made from coconut sap and known for its health benefits.

Where to Stay in Malay: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Malay offers several accommodation options to suit all budgets and preferences, from luxury resorts to budget-friendly hostels. Some popular choices include:

  • Fairways and Bluewater: A luxury resort located on the beachfront offering beautiful garden or sea views and private balconies.
  • Hampstead Boutique Hotel: A stylish boutique hotel offering comfortable rooms and modern amenities, a 10-minute drive from Boracay Island.
  • Chillax Flashpackers Hostel: A budget-friendly hostel located on Bulabog Beach, popular among backpackers and solo travelers.

What to Buy in Malay: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

Some unique local products to bring home from Malay include:

  • Patadyong Weaving: A traditional Aklanon textile made from abaca fiber or cotton and woven using a loom.
  • Sea Shells and Souvenirs: A variety of homemade souvenirs made from seashells, such as bracelets, keychains, and fridge magnets.
  • Local Food Products: Local delicacies such as piaya and barquillos.

Local Culture and Customs of Malay

The locals in Malay are friendly and welcoming, and visitors are expected to respect their customs and traditions. Here are some things to keep in mind:

  • Dress Modestly: When visiting temples or other significant cultural sites, make sure to dress modestly and cover your shoulders and knees.
  • Remove Your Shoes: It’s customary to remove your shoes when entering a local home or temple.
  • Respect the Elderly: In Filipino culture, respect for your elders is crucial, so make sure to show respect to the elderly.

Best Time to Visit Malay

The best time to visit Malay is during the dry season, between November and May. The weather is generally sunny, with little to no precipitation, making it perfect for outdoor activities and sightseeing. However, expect more significant crowds during this period, and room rates may be higher.

Do’s and Don’ts of Malay

Here are some do’s and don’ts to keep in mind while visiting Malay:

Do:

  • Respect the locals and their customs.
  • Try the local cuisine and beverages.
  • Explore the natural beauty of Malay.

Don’t:

  • Litter or disrespect the environment.
  • Be disrespectful to the locals or other tourists.
  • Forget to bring sunblock and insect repellent.

Safety and Health

Malay is generally a safe and healthy destination, with little crime or health issues. However, visitors should take the usual precautions to stay safe and healthy while traveling:

Safety in Malay

  • Avoid leaving your belongings unattended on the beach or in public areas.
  • Use caution when swimming or participating in water activities, as water currents can be strong.
  • Be aware of the local traffic, as roads can be narrow and busy.

Health in Malay

  • Always drink bottled water or boiled water to avoid getting sick from contaminated tap water.
  • Use insect repellent to prevent mosquito bites and protect yourself from diseases such as dengue fever or malaria.
  • Use sunscreen to protect your skin from the sun.

Getting to Malay: Transportation Options and Logistics

Malay is easily accessible by air, land, and sea. Here are some options for getting to Malay:

Airports Near Malay

  • Godofredo P. Ramos Airport (also known as Caticlan Airport): The closest airport to Malay and Boracay Island, with regular flights from Manila and other major cities in the Philippines.

Bus Stations Near Malay

  • Kalibo Bus Terminal: Located in Kalibo, approximately an hour and a half away from Malay, and offers buses to other major cities in the Philippines.

Other Options

  • Private Van or Car: Private vans and cars are available for hire, and they can take you directly from the airport to Malay.
  • Ferry: Ferries depart from Caticlan Jetty Port to Boracay Island, a short boat ride from Malay.

How to Get Around in Malay

The best way to explore Malay is by renting a motorbike or hiring a tricycle, a popular mode of transportation in the Philippines. Other options include:

  • Walking: The town of Malay is small and easily walkable, making it easy to explore on foot.
  • Jeepney: A colorful and unique mode of transportation used throughout the Philippines.

Where to Go Next from Malay: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

Malay is a great base for exploring nearby islands and destinations in the Philippines, including:

  • Boracay Island: One of the most popular tourist destinations in the Philippines, Boracay is known for its stunning beaches, nightlife, and water activities.
  • Antique: A sleepy province located south of Aklan, with stunning natural attractions like the Bugang River, Tibiao Falls, and Malumpati Health Spring.
  • Iloilo: A bustling city located west of Aklan, known for its rich history, delicious cuisine, and cultural attractions.

Conclusion

Malay may not be on most tourists’ radar, but it has plenty to offer visitors looking for a more relaxed and authentic Philippines experience. With its stunning natural beauty, friendly locals, and diverse cultural scene, Malay is the perfect place to unwind and explore. We hope this comprehensive guide has helped you plan your trip to Malay and make the most of your time in this charming town.
