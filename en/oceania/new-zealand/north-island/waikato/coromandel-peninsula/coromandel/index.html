Introduction to Coromandel

Coromandel, located in the Waikato region of New Zealand’s North Island, is a popular tourist destination known for its stunning coastline, pristine white sand beaches, native forests, and rugged terrain dotted with hot springs and geysers. Its Maori history, early European influence, and eco-tourism make an interesting combination, providing visitors with opportunities to experience unique cultural and natural attractions.

List of Places to Visit in Coromandel

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • The Coromandel Heritage Trust Museum: showcases the region’s history with various exhibits, ranging from early Maori settlement to European colonisation.

  • The Driving Creek Railway: a heritage railway experience that takes visitors on a scenic journey through the beautiful Coromandel hills, with informative displays depicting the history of the railway and the region.

Natural Attractions of Coromandel: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • The Coromandel Coastal Walkway: a track that winds its way around the Coromandel Peninsula through picturesque vistas of the rugged coastline, pristine beaches, and native bush.

  • The Hot Water Beach: a geothermal wonder, where visitors can dig their own hot pools in the sand and indulge in a relaxing soak, surrounded by stunning views of the Pacific Ocean.

  • The Cathedral Cove: a breathtaking natural archway that leads to a secluded beach, providing both an adventure and irresistible charm to beach lovers.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Coromandel

Coromandel is renowned for its scenic walking and hiking trails, with lush native bush, waterfalls, and scenic vistas. Here are some of the best hiking and biking routes:

  • Coromandel Walkway: a well-marked trail that traverses the Coromandel Peninsula from the west coast to the east coast. It takes around four days to complete the trail and is recommended for experienced trampers.

  • Pinnacles Walk: a challenging trail that is approximately six hours long, and takes visitors to the Pinnacles Summit with magnificent views of the forest below.

Best Things to Do in Coromandel: Activities and Tours

  • Glass Bottom Boat Tour: a scenic tour of the coastline of the Coromandel Peninsula, taking visitors through the Te Whanganui-A-Hei Marine Reserve.

  • Fishing Tour: Gather the family and experience fishing in Coromandel, and enjoy the scenic views at the Hauraki Gulf.

  • Kauri Forest Experience: an educational tour through the Waipoua Forest and a chance to see the giant kauri trees, some of which are over 2000 years old.

Food and Drink Scene of Coromandel

Must-Try Dishes

  • Fish and chips: A New Zealand classic and probably the most popular takeaway meal.

  • Green-lipped mussels: A unique type of mussel that is grown in the clean waters around New Zealand.

  • Kiwifruit: A tart and juicy fruit with an unusual hairy skin.

Local Beverages

  • Passionfruit Mojito Beer: A refreshing beer from the 303 Estate Brewery.

  • Sauvignon Blanc from Cathedral Cove Estate Winery: A delicious wine from one of the region’s many wineries.

Where to Stay in Coromandel: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

  • Anchor Lodge: An eco-friendly lodge located right on the water’s edge.

  • Oceans Resort Whitianga: A luxurious resort offering fantastic views of Mercury Bay.

  • Coromandel Top 10 Holiday Park: A budget-friendly option with a range of accommodations to suit every style and budget.

What to Buy in Coromandel: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

  • Greenstone: A precious stone deemed sacred by the Maori, and used in the creation of stunning carvings and jewelry.

  • Kiwiana products: Including uniquely New Zealand-themed T-shirts, magnets, and other traditional souvenirs.

Local Culture and Customs of Coromandel

The Maori culture is still present in Coromandel and is celebrated through annual festivals and events. Visitors to the region should respect the traditions and beliefs of local people, including being mindful of cultural protocols when visiting historic or sacred sites.

Best Time to Visit Coromandel

The best time to visit Coromandel is from November to April when the weather is warmer and dryer, enabling visitors to enjoy the many outdoor activities on offer in the region.

Do’s and Don’ts of Coromandel

Do:

  • Do bring comfortable and suitable clothing and footwear for hiking and outdoor activities.

  • Do abide by the region’s conservation and environment guidelines.

Don’t:

  • Don’t forget to take travel insurance before planning your visit.

  • Don’t pick up green-lipped mussels from the wild as it is strictly prohibited.

Safety and Health

Safety in Coromandel

Coromandel is generally considered a safe region to visit, but visitors should still follow general safety precautions, such as being aware of water conditions, wearing appropriate footwear for hiking and outdoor activities, and staying informed about weather conditions.

Health in Coromandel

Visitors to Coromandel should drink plenty of water, wear sunscreen, and take necessary precautions against insect bites.

Other Options

How to Get Around in Coromandel

Car hire is an ideal option if visitors wish to explore the region independently; however, there are also local buses and transportation options available.

Where to Go Next from Coromandel: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

  • Rotorua: A scenic and culturally significant city located within a two-hour drive from Coromandel.

  • Hobbiton: A movie location containing the Shire movie set featured in the movie Lord of the Rings located nearby in Matamata.

Conclusion

Coromandel is a diverse region of New Zealand packed with cultural and natural attractions, from hiking trails to stunning coastline and natural wonders. Its rich Maori history and European Influence make it an interesting destination for tourists of all ages, keen to explore New Zealand unique cultural roots. With its plethora of activities, delicious food and wine scene, and picturesque landscapes, Coromandel is undoubtedly one of the must-visit destinations on an itinerary to New Zealand.
