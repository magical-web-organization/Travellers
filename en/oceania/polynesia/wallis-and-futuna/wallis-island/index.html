Introduction to Wallis Island

Wallis Island is a hidden gem in the Pacific Ocean that belongs to the French overseas collectivity of Wallis and Futuna. This Polynesian island, also known as Uvea, is located halfway between Hawaii and New Zealand. It is a remote paradise with crystal-clear waters, lush forests, and fascinating culture that is waiting to be discovered by adventurous travelers.

The island’s population is around 10,000 people, most of whom are of Polynesian descent and speak French and Wallisian, the local language. The pristine nature and rich cultural heritage of Wallis Island make it an ideal destination for those seeking an authentic Polynesian experience.

List of Places to Visit in Wallis Island

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • Museum of Wallis and Futuna: This museum is located in Mata Utu, the capital of Wallis and Futuna, and offers a wealth of information about the island’s history and culture. It houses traditional Polynesian artifacts, ancient tools, and weapons, and various exhibits that showcase the island’s rich cultural heritage.

  • Lalolalo Lake: This beautiful crater lake is one of the most iconic landmarks of Wallis Island. It is considered sacred by the locals and is believed to be the dwelling place of ancestral spirits.

  • Sacred Heart Cathedral: This impressive cathedral is located in Mata Utu and is one of the most significant religious buildings on Wallis Island. Its unique architecture and ornate interior are a reflection of the island’s European and Polynesian influences.

  • Fort Tonga: Built by the French in the 19th century, Fort Tonga is a historical monument that provides a glimpse into the island’s colonial past. The fort is perched on a hill and offers panoramic views of the surrounding landscape.

Natural Attractions of Wallis Island: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • Lake Lalolalo: Lalolalo Lake is a beautiful crater lake that is believed to be sacred by the locals. It is surrounded by lush forests and is a popular destination for hiking and fishing.

  • Mala’efo’ou Park: This picturesque park is situated in Mata Utu and offers a range of outdoor activities, including basketball, football, and volleyball. It is also an ideal spot for picnics and family outings.

  • Loto Ta’alo: This stunning natural swimming pool is located in Mu’a, and its crystal-clear waters provide a refreshing break from the tropical heat. It is surrounded by lush vegetation, and its secluded location makes it a peaceful hideaway.

  • Mount Lulu Fakahega: This towering peak is the highest point on Wallis Island, rising to a height of 524 meters above sea level. It offers breathtaking panoramic views of the surrounding landscape and is a popular destination for hiking and trekking.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Wallis Island

Wallis Island is a paradise for outdoor enthusiasts, and its lush forests and stunning coastline provide ample opportunities for hiking and cycling. Some of the most popular trails on the island are:

  • Mount Lulu Fakahega Trail: This challenging hike takes you to the summit of Wallis Island’s highest peak and offers breathtaking views of the surrounding landscape. The trail is steep and rocky, but the reward is worth the effort.

  • Lalolalo Lake Trail: This beautiful trail takes you around Lalolalo Lake, one of the island’s most iconic landmarks. It is an easy hike and is suitable for all skill levels.

  • Mu’a Coastal Trail: This picturesque trail takes you along the scenic coastline of Mu’a and showcases some of the island’s most stunning natural attractions. It is a moderate hike that can be completed in a few hours.

Best Things to Do in Wallis Island: Activities and Tours

  • Traditional Dance Show: Wallisian culture is known for its unique dance and music traditions, and attending a traditional dance show is a must-do activity for visitors to the island.

  • Snorkeling and Scuba Diving: The crystal-clear waters surrounding Wallis Island are teeming with marine life, making it an ideal destination for snorkeling and scuba diving. Some of the best spots for diving on the island are the coral reefs of Lavegahau and Alofi.

  • Island Tour: An island tour is a great way to explore the natural and cultural attractions of Wallis Island. You can hire a local guide who can take you to the island’s most scenic spots and provide insights into the local culture.

Week-Long Itinerary for Wallis Island

Day 1: Arrive at Mata Utu and check into your hotel. Visit the Museum of Wallis and Futuna. Day 2: Take a hike around Lalolalo Lake and visit the Sacred Heart Cathedral in the afternoon. Day 3: Explore the Mu’a Coastal Trail and relax at Loto Ta’alo natural pool. Day 4: Take a day trip to Alofi Island and snorkel in its crystal-clear waters. Day 5: Visit Fort Tonga and spend the afternoon at Mala’efo’ou Park. Day 6: Go scuba diving in the coral reefs of Lavegahau and attend a traditional dance show in the evening. Day 7: Spend the day at Mount Lulu Fakahega, hiking to its summit and enjoying the panoramic views.

Food and Drink Scene of Wallis Island

Must-Try Dishes

  • Laulau: A traditional Polynesian dish that consists of pork, coconut cream, and taro leaves wrapped in banana leaves and steamed.

  • Ufi: A starchy root vegetable that is commonly used in Polynesian cooking. It is often mashed and served with coconut cream.

  • Poisson Cru: A local specialty made from fresh raw fish, lime juice, coconut cream, and vegetables.

Local Beverages

  • Kava: A traditional Polynesian drink made from the roots of the kava plant. It has a mild sedative effect and is often consumed during social gatherings.

  • Coconut Milk: Coconut milk is a popular beverage on Wallis Island and is often used in cooking as well. It is refreshing and rich in nutrients.

Where to Stay in Wallis Island: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Wallis Island has a limited number of accommodation options, but visitors can choose from a range of hotels, guesthouses, and hostels.

  • Le Lomipeau: A budget-friendly guesthouse located in Mata Utu, offering comfortable rooms and free Wi-Fi.

  • Mauli Lodge: A boutique hotel located in Hahake, offering spacious rooms and a range of amenities, including a swimming pool and restaurant.

  • Hotel Gouverneur: A luxury hotel located in Mata Utu, offering elegant rooms and great views of the ocean.

What to Buy in Wallis Island: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

  • Tapa Cloth: Tapa cloth is a traditional Polynesian fabric made from tree bark and is often adorned with intricate designs and patterns.

  • Pearls: Wallis Island is known for its beautiful pearls, which are cultivated in the lagoons surrounding the island. They come in a range of colors and sizes and make great souvenirs.

  • Handmade Crafts: Local artisans on Wallis Island produce a range of handmade crafts, including woven baskets and mats, carved woodwork, and shell jewelry.

Local Culture and Customs of Wallis Island

Wallis Island has a rich cultural heritage, which is reflected in its art, music, and dance traditions. Visitors are encouraged to respect the local customs and traditions, including dress codes and social etiquette.

  • Traditional Dress: Men are expected to dress conservatively, wearing a shirt and long pants, while women are expected to cover their shoulders and knees.

  • Respecting Ancestral Tombs: Wallis Island’s ancestral tombs are considered sacred, and visitors are asked to respect them by not touching or disturbing them.

Best Time to Visit Wallis Island

The best time to visit Wallis Island is between May and October when the weather is dry and sunny. The island has a tropical climate, and temperatures remain warm throughout the year, with occasional rainfall and strong winds.

Do’s and Don’ts of Wallis Island

Do:

  • Respect the Local Culture: Visitors are encouraged to respect the local customs and traditions, including dress codes and social etiquette.

  • Try Local Cuisine: Wallis Island’s cuisine is a unique fusion of Polynesian and European influences, and visitors are encouraged to try local dishes during their stay.

Don’t:

  • Disrespect Ancestral Tombs: Wallis Island’s ancestral tombs are considered sacred, and visitors are asked to respect them by not touching or disturbing them.

  • Overstay Your Visa: Visitors to Wallis Island are required to have a valid visa, and overstaying your visa can result in fines or deportation.

Safety and Health

Safety in Wallis Island

Wallis Island is considered a safe destination for visitors, with low crime rates and a friendly local population. However, visitors are advised to take normal precautions, such as avoiding isolated areas and not carrying large sums of money with them.

Health in Wallis Island

Visitors to Wallis Island are advised to take precautions against mosquito-borne illnesses, such as dengue fever and Zika virus. It is recommended to use insect repellent, wear protective clothing, and sleep under mosquito nets.

Getting to Wallis Island: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Wallis Island

Wallis Island has an international airport, Hihifo Airport, which is located in the village of Hihifo and is served by Air Calin.

Bus Stations Near Wallis Island

Wallis Island does not have a public bus system, but visitors can hire taxis or rent a car to get around the island.

Other Options

There are no other transportation options to reach Wallis Island, and visitors are advised to plan their travel well in advance.

How to Get Around in Wallis Island

The best way to get around Wallis Island is by car or taxi, which can be hired at the airport or in Mata Utu. Visitors can also rent bicycles or motorbikes to explore the island on their own.

Where to Go Next from Wallis Island: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

Wallis Island is surrounded by beautiful lagoons and islets that are waiting to be explored. Some of the nearby destinations and excursions include:

  • Futuna Island: Futuna Island is located just 40 km from Wallis Island and is another beautiful Polynesian destination that is worth exploring.

  • Alofi Island: Alofi Island is a small, uninhabited island located just off the coast of Wallis Island. It is a popular destination for snorkeling and diving.

Conclusion

Wallis Island is a hidden gem in the Pacific Ocean that offers visitors a unique blend of pristine nature and rich cultural heritage. With its stunning beaches, lush forests, and fascinating history, this Polynesian paradise is a must-visit destination for anyone seeking an authentic and unforgettable travel experience.
