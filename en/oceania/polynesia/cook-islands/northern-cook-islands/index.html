Introduction to Northern Cook Islands

Northern Cook Islands is a group of six atolls and one raised coral island in the South Pacific Ocean. It is a self-governing territory of New Zealand and has a population of around 3,500 people. The islands boast a laid-back lifestyle, pristine beaches, turquoise lagoons, and a rich Polynesian culture that dates back over 1,500 years. This travel guide will help you explore the best of the Northern Cook Islands.

List of Places to Visit in Northern Cook Islands

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

The Cook Islands Library and Museum

The Cook Islands Library and Museum in Avarua, Rarotonga, is a treasure trove of traditional Maori artifacts, including adzes, war clubs, and ceremonial drums. The museum also houses a collection of books, maps, and photographs that detail the history of the islands.

Arai-Te-Tonga (The Stone of the South)

Arai-Te-Tonga is a large, flat coral rock in the village of Taputapuatea, Aitutaki. It is where the first Polynesian king, Tangiia Nui, landed his canoe, Vaka Takitumu, in the 14th century. The stone is considered a sacred site and is one of the most significant historical landmarks on the island.

Natural Attractions of Northern Cook Islands: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

One Foot Island

One Foot Island, located in Aitutaki Lagoon, is a stunning white sand beach with turquoise waters. The island is part of the Aitutaki Marine Park, a protected area that is home to over 400 species of fish. The snorkeling here is exceptional, with vibrant coral reefs and an abundance of colorful marine life.

Te Rua Manga (The Needle)

Te Rua Manga is a 413-meter tall peak that dominates the landscape of Rarotonga. The trek to the summit takes around three hours and is challenging but rewarding, with stunning views of the island and its lagoons.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Northern Cook Islands

Rarotonga Cross-Island Trek

The Rarotonga Cross-Island Trek is a challenging hike that takes you through lush rainforests, over rocky ridges, and down to waterfalls and swimming holes. The trail starts at Avatiu Valley and ends at Wigmore’s Waterfall in the interior of the island.

Aitutaki Mountain Bike Tour

The Aitutaki Mountain Bike Tour is a four-hour guided tour that takes you around the island, visiting the best beaches, viewpoints, and cultural sites. It includes stops at One Foot Island, the Aitutaki Lagoon Resort, and the Aitutaki Golf Course.

Best Things to Do in Northern Cook Islands: Activities and Tours

Cultural Shows

The cultural shows in the Northern Cook Islands are a must-see, featuring traditional dancing, drumming, and singing. The shows also include demonstrations of weaving, carving, and cooking, giving visitors a glimpse into the ancient Polynesian culture of the islands.

Scuba Diving

The Northern Cook Islands are a world-renowned scuba diving destination. The crystal-clear waters of the lagoons offer excellent visibility, and the reefs are home to an abundance of marine life, including sharks, rays, and turtles.

Food and Drink Scene of Northern Cook Islands

Must-Try Dishes

Ika Mata

Ika Mata is a traditional Polynesian dish that consists of raw fish marinated in coconut cream, lime juice, and onions. It is usually served as an appetizer or a main course and is a popular dish throughout the Northern Cook Islands.

Rukau

Rukau is a traditional side dish made from taro leaves, grated coconut, and onions. It is often served with fish, chicken, or pork and is a staple of the local diet.

Local Beverages

Kava

Kava is a traditional drink made from the root of the kava plant. It has mild sedative properties and is often served at social gatherings and ceremonies in the Northern Cook Islands.

Tumunu

Tumunu is a traditional gathering around a kava bowl and is a significant part of the Polynesian culture. It is considered a sacred event and is a chance for the community to come together and share stories and traditions.

Where to Stay in Northern Cook Islands: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Aitutaki Lagoon Private Island Resort

The Aitutaki Lagoon Private Island Resort is a luxury resort on a private island in Aitutaki Lagoon. It features overwater bungalows, beachfront villas, a restaurant, a spa, and access to a secluded beach.

Paradise Inn

The Paradise Inn is a budget-friendly guesthouse in Rarotonga. It is located close to the airport and features comfortable rooms, a restaurant, and a bar.

What to Buy in Northern Cook Islands: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

The Northern Cook Islands offer several unique souvenirs and local products that are perfect for bringing home as a reminder of your trip. These include:

  • Pareu (sarongs)
  • Tivaivai (traditional patchwork quilts)
  • Black pearls
  • Taro chips
  • Coconut oil

Local Culture and Customs of Northern Cook Islands

The Northern Cook Islands have a rich Polynesian culture that is deeply ingrained in the daily lives of the locals. Visitors are encouraged to respect local customs and traditions, including:

  • Removing shoes before entering a home or sacred site
  • Covering up when visiting churches or religious sites
  • Asking for permission before taking photographs of people or cultural events

Best Time to Visit Northern Cook Islands

The best time to visit the Northern Cook Islands is during the dry season, which runs from May to October. The weather is mild, and there is less chance of rain. However, this is also the high season, and prices for accommodation and tours can be more expensive.

Do’s and Don’ts of Northern Cook Islands

Do:

  • Embrace the local culture and traditions
  • Enjoy the natural beauty of the islands
  • Take part in cultural events and activities

Don’t:

  • Take coral from the beaches
  • Leave trash or litter on the beaches or in the water
  • Disrespect local customs or traditions

Safety and Health

Safety in Northern Cook Islands

The Northern Cook Islands are generally safe for visitors. However, it is still advisable to take common sense precautions, such as locking your room and not leaving valuables unattended on the beach.

Health in Northern Cook Islands

There are no significant health risks in the Northern Cook Islands, and no vaccines are required to visit. However, it is important to take precautions against mosquito bites and to drink bottled or boiled water.

Getting to Northern Cook Islands: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Northern Cook Islands

Rarotonga International Airport is the main airport in the Northern Cook Islands. It is served by Air New Zealand, Virgin Australia, and Jetstar, with direct flights from Auckland, Sydney, and Los Angeles.

Bus Stations Near Northern Cook Islands

The Northern Cook Islands do not have a bus system. Taxis, rental cars, and scooters are available for hire on the main islands.

Other Options

The Northern Cook Islands can also be visited by cruise ship. P&O Cruises and Princess Cruises both visit the islands as part of their South Pacific itineraries.

How to Get Around in Northern Cook Islands

The best way to get around the Northern Cook Islands is by rental car or scooter. Both are readily available on the main islands and provide the flexibility to explore at your own pace.

Where to Go Next from Northern Cook Islands: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

Samoa

Samoa is an independent country located around 2,500 km east of the Northern Cook Islands. It is known for its stunning beaches, waterfalls, and adventure activities such as snorkeling, diving, and hiking.

Fiji

Fiji is an archipelago of over 300 islands located around 2,000 km northwest of the Northern Cook Islands. It is a popular tourist destination, with white sand beaches, coral reefs, and a vibrant local culture.

Conclusion

The Northern Cook Islands offer a unique blend of natural beauty, cultural heritage, and Polynesian charm. Whether you’re looking for adventure, relaxation, or a cultural experience, the islands have something to offer. Use this comprehensive travel guide to plan your itinerary and make the most of your trip to the Northern Cook Islands.
