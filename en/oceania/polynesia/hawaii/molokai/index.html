Introduction to Molokai

Molokai is a small island located in Hawaii, belonging to the Polynesia region in Oceania. The island is known for its untouched natural beauty, its rich cultural heritage, and its close-knit community that values its traditions and customs. Molokai is an ideal destination for travelers who seek to disconnect from the hustle and bustle of city life and experience a place where time seems to have stood still. In this guide, we present everything you need to know to plan your perfect trip to Molokai.

List of Places to Visit in Molokai

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

Molokai preserves its cultural heritage through a rich variety of museums and historical places that visitors can explore:

  • Molokai Museum and Cultural Center: This is one of the best places to learn about the history and traditions of Molokai. The museum houses artifacts, documents, and photographs that illustrate the island’s past, including the experience of the local community during the era of sugar plantations.
  • Kalaupapa National Historical Park: This park is a tribute to the thousands of people who were exiled to Kalaupapa due to the leprosy epidemic in the 19th and 20th centuries. Visitors can take a tour and learn about the lives of the patients who lived in the area.
  • St. Joseph’s Church: This church was built in 1876 and features a striking facade made of coral rock and lime. The interior is decorated with frescoes and stained-glass windows that reflect the devotion of the community to Catholicism.

Natural Attractions of Molokai: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

Molokai offers visitors an array of natural attractions that are perfect for outdoor enthusiasts:

  • Papohaku Beach: This is one of the most beautiful beaches in Hawaii, with three miles of white sand and crystal-clear water. The beach is perfect for swimming, sunbathing, and watching the sunset.
  • Halawa Valley: This valley is home to several waterfalls, including the Mooula Falls, which drops over 150 feet. Visitors can hike to the falls and swim in the natural pool at the base.
  • Kalaupapa Lookout: This lookout point offers panoramic views of the Pacific Ocean and the Molokai coastline. On clear days, visitors can see as far as Maui and Lanai.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Molokai

Molokai is a haven for hikers and bikers, with miles of scenic trails that allow visitors to explore the island’s natural wonders:

  • Halawa Valley Trail: This trail takes visitors through the lush vegetation of Halawa Valley, past waterfalls, and taro fields.
  • Kalaupapa Trail: This trail is a challenging hike that takes visitors to the Kalaupapa peninsula, where the leper colony was located. The trail features stunning views of the ocean and the cliffs along the coast.
  • Mooula Falls Trail: This trail is a moderate hike that leads to the Mooula Falls. The trail passes through bamboo forests and leads to a natural pool where visitors can swim.

Best Things to Do in Molokai: Activities and Tours

Molokai offers visitors an array of activities and tours that cater to different interests and tastes:

  • Whale Watching: Visitors can take a boat tour during the whale-watching season (December to May) and see humpback whales in their natural habitat.
  • Mule Ride to Kalaupapa: Visitors can take a mule ride down the Kalaupapa Trail and learn about the history of the leper colony. The ride features stunning views of the ocean and the cliffs along the coast.
  • Snorkeling and Scuba Diving: Molokai offers some of the best snorkeling and scuba diving spots in Hawaii, with colorful coral reefs and an abundance of marine life.

Week-Long Itinerary for Molokai

If you’re planning a week-long trip to Molokai, here’s a sample itinerary to help you get started:

Day Activity
1 Visit the Molokai Museum and Cultural Center, and learn about the island’s history.
2 Hike the Halawa Valley trail and visit the Mooula Falls.
3 Take a mule ride to Kalaupapa and learn about the leper colony.
4 Explore the Papohaku Beach and watch the sunset.
5 Take a boat tour and go whale watching.
6 Snorkel or scuba dive in the coral reefs.
7 Visit the Kalaupapa National Historical Park and St. Joseph’s Church.

Food and Drink Scene of Molokai

Must-Try Dishes

Molokai’s cuisine is a blend of Hawaiian, Polynesian, and Asian influences, with fresh ingredients from the land and the sea. Some of the must-try dishes are:

  • Kalua Pork: This traditional Hawaiian dish is made by roasting a whole pig in an underground oven and shredding the meat. It’s usually served with rice and poi, a Hawaiian staple made from mashed taro root.
  • Poke: This dish consists of marinated raw fish, usually ahi tuna, served with seaweed, onions, and soy sauce. It’s a fresh and flavorful dish that reflects the island’s connection to the ocean.
  • Malasadas: These are Portuguese donuts that have become a popular dessert in Hawaii. They’re usually dusted with sugar and served warm.

Local Beverages

Molokai is home to a variety of local beverages that visitors should try:

  • Kona Coffee: While Kona coffee is grown on the Big Island, it’s widely available on Molokai and has a rich and smooth flavor.
  • Mai Tai: This is a tropical cocktail made with rum, orange curacao, lime juice, and orgeat syrup. It’s a refreshing and potent drink that’s perfect for a day by the beach.
  • Lilikoi Juice: This juice is made from passionfruit and has a tangy and sweet flavor. It’s a refreshing drink that’s perfect for hot days.

Where to Stay in Molokai: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Molokai offers visitors a range of accommodations that cater to different budgets and preferences:

  • Hotel Molokai: This hotel is located in Kaunakakai and offers guests comfortable rooms and an outdoor pool. It’s a great option for those who want to be centrally located.
  • Molokai Vacation Properties: This company manages a variety of rental properties on the island, from apartments to beachfront villas. It’s a good option for those who want more privacy and space.
  • Hula Shores: This hostel is located in the east end of the island and offers dormitory-style accommodation. It’s a budget-friendly option for backpackers and solo travelers.

What to Buy in Molokai: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

Molokai offers visitors a variety of souvenirs and local products that make great gifts:

  • Hawaiian Quilts: These quilts feature colorful and intricate designs that reflect the island’s natural beauty. They make great wall hangings or bedspreads.
  • Macadamia Nuts: Hawaii is known for its delicious macadamia nuts, and Molokai produces some of the best. They’re available in a variety of flavors, from plain to chocolate-covered.
  • Hawaiian Salt: This salt is harvested from the ocean and blended with red alaea clay, which gives it a distinctive pink color. It’s a versatile seasoning that adds a touch of Hawaii to any dish.

Local Culture and Customs of Molokai

Molokai has a rich culture that’s still alive and well, and visitors should respect local customs and traditions:

  • Dress Appropriately: When visiting cultural or religious sites, dress modestly and remove your shoes if required.
  • Respect Privacy: Molokai is a close-knit community, and visitors should respect the privacy of locals and their property.
  • Be Polite: Hawaiian culture values harmony and respect, and visitors should be polite and courteous at all times.

Best Time to Visit Molokai

The best time to visit Molokai is between May and September, when the weather is dry and sunny. However, keep in mind that this is also peak tourist season, and prices may be higher. If you’re looking for a quieter and more affordable trip, consider visiting between October and April.

Do’s and Don’ts of Molokai

Do:

  • Do Take Your Time: Molokai is a place to slow down and enjoy the simple things in life. Take the time to savor the scenery, the food, and the local culture.
  • Do Respect Nature: Molokai’s natural beauty is its most valuable asset. Practice sustainable tourism and leave no trace when exploring the island.
  • Do Learn About Local Culture: Molokai has a rich cultural heritage that’s worth exploring. Attend festivals, visit museums, and learn about the traditions and customs of the local community.

Don’t:

  • Don’t Be Loud or Disruptive: Molokai values tranquility and peace. Avoid loud music, parties, or other activities that may disturb the local community.
  • Don’t Take Anything from the Beach: Molokai’s beaches are protected areas, and taking shells or other natural artifacts is strictly prohibited.
  • Don’t Use Drugs or Alcohol in Public: Hawaii has strict laws regarding drug and alcohol use, and visitors should respect local regulations.

Safety and Health

Safety in Molokai

Molokai is a safe destination, but visitors should take the usual precautions to avoid accidents or theft:

  • Stay on Marked Trails: Molokai’s natural areas can be challenging, and visitors should stay on marked trails to avoid accidents or getting lost.
  • Lock Your Car: Car theft is not common in Molokai, but visitors should take basic precautions such as locking their car and not leaving valuables inside.
  • Watch Out for Wildlife: Molokai is home to a variety of wildlife, including wild boars and mosquitoes. Visitors should take the necessary precautions to avoid bites or encounters.

Health in Molokai

Molokai has a small hospital and a few clinics that offer basic medical care. Visitors should have travel insurance that covers medical emergencies and be aware of the following health issues:

  • Sunburn: Molokai is sunny and warm, and visitors should use sunblock and avoid prolonged exposure to the sun.
  • Mosquito-Borne Diseases: Dengue fever and Zika virus are not common in Molokai, but visitors should take precautions against mosquito bites.
  • Seafood Allergies: Molokai has a rich seafood culture, and visitors with allergies should be cautious when trying new dishes.

Getting to Molokai: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Molokai

Molokai has one airport, the Molokai Airport (MKK), located in Hoolehua. The airport offers flights to Honolulu and Kahului on Maui.

Bus Stations Near Molokai

Molokai has a public bus service called the Molokai MEO Bus, which connects the main towns on the island. However, the service is limited, and visitors may find it more convenient to rent a car or take a taxi.

Other Options

Visitors can also reach Molokai by ferry from Lahaina on Maui. The ferry service is operated by the Molokai Princess.

How to Get Around in Molokai

The best way to get around Molokai is by car, which can be rented at the airport or in town. Visitors should be aware that Molokai has only one main road, the Kamehameha V Highway, which connects the main towns on the island. The speed limit is 45 mph, and visitors should watch out for pedestrians, cyclists, and wildlife.

Where to Go Next from Molokai: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

Molokai is a great base for exploring other destinations in Hawaii, including:

  • Maui: Molokai’s neighbor island is known for its pristine beaches, its famous Road to Hana, and its lively resort towns.
  • Honolulu: The capital of Hawaii offers visitors a rich variety of cultural attractions, from the Pearl Harbor Memorial to the Bishop Museum.
  • Lanai: This small island is known for its luxury resorts and its world-class golf courses.

Conclusion

Molokai is a unique and enchanting destination that offers visitors a chance to experience Hawaii in a different way. From its natural wonders to its cultural heritage, Molokai has something for everyone. We hope this guide has been helpful in planning your trip to this beautiful island. So pack your bags and hit the road to experience the natural beauty, history, and tranquility of Molokai.
