Introduction to Daru

Daru is a small town in Western Papua New Guinea, located on an island of the same name. With its stunning natural beauty and rich cultural heritage, Daru is a hidden gem that is still relatively unexplored by tourists. The town is home to a diverse community of people, including the Daru Islanders, who have a unique culture and way of life.

In this guide, we’ll take a closer look at the best places to visit, things to do, where to stay, what to buy, local culture and customs, safety and health, and more.

List of Places to Visit in Daru

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • Daru Cultural Museum: This museum showcases the history and culture of the Daru Islanders, with exhibits on traditional costumes, music, dance, and more.
  • St. Mary’s Chapel: A historic church that was built by the Marist missionaries in the early 20th century.
  • Daru Fort: This fort was built in the late 1800s by the British colonial government to protect the island from attack.

Natural Attractions of Daru: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • Daru Island Beaches: With its crystal-clear waters and white sandy beaches, Daru Island is the perfect spot for sunbathing, swimming, and snorkeling.
  • Fly River: The Fly River is the longest river in Papua New Guinea, and offers breathtaking views of the surrounding rainforest and mountain ranges.
  • Bat Cave: The Bat Cave is a natural wonder that is home to thousands of bats. Visitors can take a guided tour of the cave and observe the bats in their natural habitat.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Daru

  • Daru Island Coastal Trail: This trail offers stunning views of the ocean and takes visitors through the island’s lush rainforest.
  • Fly River Trek: This trek takes visitors through the remote wilderness of Western Papua New Guinea, and offers a chance to see some of the region’s most unique wildlife.

Best Things to Do in Daru: Activities and Tours

  • Fishing Charters: Daru is known for its excellent fishing, and visitors can book fishing charters to catch tuna, marlin, and other local fish.
  • Cultural Tours: Visitors can take guided tours of the island to learn about the local culture and way of life.
  • Snorkeling and Diving: The waters around Daru Island are home to a variety of marine life, including colorful fish, sea turtles, and coral reefs.

Food and Drink Scene of Daru

Must-Try Dishes

  • Sago: Sago is a staple food in Papua New Guinea, made from the extract of the sago palm. It can be eaten alone or with other foods.
  • Coconut Cream Fish: Fish cooked in coconut cream is a popular dish in Daru.
  • Pangi: Pangi is a traditional Daru Island dish made from taro, fish, and coconut cream.

Local Beverages

  • Betel Nut: Betel nut is a stimulant that is chewed by locals. Visitors can try it, but be warned that it can cause staining of the teeth.
  • Coconut Water: Coconut water is a refreshing drink that is widely available on the island.
  • Homebrew Beer: Locals brew their own beer, which can be found in some of the local bars and restaurants.

Where to Stay in Daru: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

  • Daru Lodge: This hotel offers comfortable rooms with stunning views of the ocean.
  • Island Breeze Lodge: A budget-friendly option that offers basic accommodations in a central location.
  • Daru Guesthouse: This guesthouse offers a home-away-from-home experience, with friendly hosts and comfortable rooms.

What to Buy in Daru: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

  • Carvings: Wood carvings and sculptures can be found in the local markets.
  • Shell Jewelry: Locally-made jewelry using shells and other natural materials can be found in the markets.
  • Papua New Guinea Art: Traditional art, including paintings and masks, can be found in the local markets.

Local Culture and Customs of Daru

The Daru Islanders have a unique culture and way of life, and visitors should take the time to learn about their customs and traditions. Some of the important aspects of their culture include:

  • Initiations: Young men undergo initiation ceremonies to mark their transition into adulthood.
  • Sing-Sings: Traditional dance and music performances are an important part of Daru Island culture.
  • Tapas: The tapa is a traditional cloth that is made from the bark of a tree and decorated with intricate designs.

Best Time to Visit Daru

The best time to visit Daru is during the dry season, which runs from May to October. This is the ideal time for outdoor activities, as there is less rain and the temperatures are more comfortable.

Do’s and Don’ts of Daru

Do:

  • Do dress modestly, especially when visiting religious sites.
  • Do respect the local culture and customs.
  • Do try the local cuisine and beverages.

Don’t:

  • Don’t take photographs without asking permission.
  • Don’t bring drugs into the country.
  • Don’t touch or disturb the wildlife.

Safety and Health

Safety in Daru

Visitors should exercise caution when travelling in Western Papua New Guinea, as crime and civil unrest can be an issue. It is important to check the latest travel advisories and to take appropriate safety precautions.

Health in Daru

Visitors should take precautions to avoid mosquito bites, as malaria and other mosquito-borne diseases are prevalent in the region. It is also important to drink only bottled or boiled water to avoid waterborne illnesses.

Getting to Daru: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Daru

  • Daru Airport: The main airport serving Daru Island, with flights from Port Moresby and other regional hubs.

Bus Stations Near Daru

  • There are no bus stations on Daru Island.

Other Options

  • Private Boat: Visitors can charter a private boat to take them to Daru Island from the mainland.

How to Get Around in Daru

The best way to get around Daru Island is by foot, as the island is small and easy to navigate. Taxis and rental cars are also available for those who prefer not to walk.

Where to Go Next from Daru: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

  • Kiunga: Located on the mainland, Kiunga is a popular destination for birdwatching and exploring the nearby rainforest.
  • Port Moresby: Papua New Guinea’s capital city is a bustling hub of culture and commerce, with plenty of sightseeing opportunities.

Conclusion

Daru is a hidden gem that is just waiting to be discovered by adventurous travelers. With its stunning natural beauty, rich cultural heritage, and vibrant local community, Daru Island is a destination that is not to be missed. Use this comprehensive guide to plan your trip to Daru and make the most of your time on this beautiful island.
