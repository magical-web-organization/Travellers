Introduction to Parque 93

Parque 93 is an upscale district in Bogotá, Colombia, which is best known for its array of chic bars, restaurants, and some of the most beautiful outdoor spaces in the city. It is situated in the upscale Andino area of the city and has a variety of experiences to offer visitors. Parque 93 is a beautiful local ecosystem that tourists can enjoy. From natural parks and protected forests to gourmet restaurants and cafes, Parque 93 is a hidden paradise that is waiting to be explored.

Local Culture and Customs of Parque 93

  • Colombians are usually friendly and welcoming to tourists.
  • It is essential to learn a few words in Spanish.
  • Smoking is prohibited in public parks and enclosed public spaces.

Best Time to Visit Parque 93

The best time to visit Parque 93 is in the dry season, which is from December to February. The temperatures are moderate, and the sky is clear, which makes it perfect for hiking or exploring the city. The rainy season runs from March to May and from September to November, so it is essential to prepare for this if you are visiting during these months.

Do’s and Don’ts of Parque 93

Do:

  • Dress appropriately for the weather and activities planned.
  • Learn some Spanish phrases.
  • Shop at local stores and markets.

Don’t:

  • Don’t walk alone at night.
  • Do not accept food or drinks from strangers.
  • Don’t engage in illegal drug use.

Safety and Health

Safety in Parque 93

The area around Parque 93 is generally safe, but pickpockets and muggings are common. Usage of public transport after dark is discouraged. Tourists need to be extra vigilant about their belongings, especially when visiting crowded areas or tourist attractions. Always use licensed taxis and pre-booked tour providers.

Health in Parque 93

Visitors are highly recommended to take out comprehensive travel insurance. Although Bogotá is a well-developed city in terms of health care facilities, visitors are advised to take precautions against altitude sickness as the district lies between 2600 and 3000 meters above sea level.
