Introduction to Tunja

Tunja is a charming city located in the department of Boyacá in Colombia, South America. It is known for its rich history, cultural heritage, and beautiful scenery. Tunja was founded in the 16th century and served as an important center for the Spanish colonial authorities. Today, the city features museums, historical landmarks, natural parks, and outdoor activities that attract tourists from all over the world.

List of Places to Visit in Tunja

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

Tunja is a city steeped in history and culture. Tourists can visit a number of museums and historical landmarks, including:

  • Museo del Carmen: This museum features exhibits on religious art, pre-Columbian artifacts, and the history of Boyacá. It also houses the remains of the first bishop of Tunja, Juan de las Barrios.

  • Casa del Fundador: This 17th-century colonial house belonged to the founder of Tunja, Captain Gonzalo Suárez Rendón. Today, it serves as a cultural center and museum.

  • Catedral de Tunja: This cathedral dates back to the 16th century and features an impressive Gothic-style façade. It is located in the main square of the city and is a popular spot for visitors and locals alike.

  • Monumento a los Lanceros: This monument commemorates the battle of Boyacá, which marked the beginning of Colombia’s struggle for independence. It features a statue of Simón Bolívar and his troops on horseback.

Natural Attractions of Tunja: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

Tunja is surrounded by beautiful natural landscapes and offers a variety of outdoor activities, including:

  • Parque de la Vida: This park is located in the heart of Tunja and features a playground, a lake, walking paths, and a botanical garden. It is a popular spot for picnics and family outings.

  • Páramo de Iguaque: This natural park is located just outside Tunja and features forests, waterfalls, and hiking trails. The park is home to a variety of wildlife, including the Colombian spectacled bear.

  • Laguna de Tota: This is the largest lake in Colombia and is located about an hour from Tunja. Visitors can enjoy activities such as boating, fishing, and swimming while admiring the stunning views of the surrounding mountains.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Tunja

Tunja is a great place for outdoor enthusiasts and has many hiking and biking routes to explore. Some of the popular ones include:

  • Camino Real de Guane: This is a 7-kilometer trail that leads to the town of Guane. It features stunning views of the countryside and the Andes Mountains.

  • Páramo de Ocetá: This is a challenging hike that takes visitors through the Páramo de Ocetá natural park. The trail features unique flora and fauna, including the frailejón plant.

  • Ciudad de Piedra: This is a 13-kilometer trail that leads to a fascinating rock formation known as Ciudad de Piedra. The hike is moderately difficult and offers stunning views of the surrounding canyons and valleys.

Best Things to Do in Tunja: Activities and Tours

Besides visiting museums and natural attractions, Tunja offers visitors a wide range of activities and tours.

  • Hot Springs: Close to the city, visitors can take a dip in hot springs. Some of the most popular include Termales de Paipa and Termales de Iza.

  • Coffee Tour: Hacienda Coloma, just outside Tunja, offers visitors the chance to learn about the coffee-making process and sample some of the best Colombian coffee.

  • Go Horseback Riding: Horseback riding is a great way to explore the surrounding countryside. Visitors can go on guided tours to places, such as the town of Nobsa and the Iguaque National Park.

Week-Long Itinerary for Tunja

Visitors can enjoy a full week of activities in Tunja. Here is a suggested itinerary:

  • Day 1: Visit the Museo del Carmen and Casa del Fundador, and explore the historic center of the city.
  • Day 2: Go hiking in the Páramo de Ocetá or explore the nearby town of Villa de Leyva.
  • Day 3: Visit the Catedral de Tunja and go horseback riding on a guided tour.
  • Day 4: Take a coffee tour of Hacienda Coloma and enjoy a relaxing afternoon at Termales de Paipa.
  • Day 5: Visit the Monumento a los Lanceros and explore Ciudad de Piedra.
  • Day 6: Spend the day at Laguna de Tota, boating, and swimming.
  • Day 7: Relax in the Parque de la Vida and discover the local food scene.

Food and Drink Scene of Tunja

Tunja’s cuisine is a mixture of traditional Andean and Spanish dishes, with some modern twists. Visitors can try a variety of local specialties.

Must-Try Dishes

  • Ajiaco Santafereño: This hearty soup is made with three types of potatoes, chicken, corn, and spices. It’s a staple dish in the region and is served with avocado and capers.

  • Fritanga Boyacense: This dish is a platter of grilled meats, chorizo, morcilla (blood sausage), and arepa (corn cake). It’s often served with potatoes, avocado, and criolla sauce (a mixture of onions, tomatoes, and cilantro).

  • Lechona: This dish is a whole roasted pig stuffed with rice, peas, onion, and spices. It’s a typical dish during celebrations and is usually served with chimichurri sauce.

Local Beverages

  • Chicha: This fermented corn drink is a traditional beverage in the Andean region. It’s often served with empanadas or sweet bread.

  • Aguardiente: This is the national drink of Colombia and is a type of anise-flavored liquor. It’s often served in shots and is a favorite drink during celebrations.

Where to Stay in Tunja: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Tunja has a variety of accommodation options to suit all budgets and needs.

  • Hotel Hunza: This hotel is located in the historic center of Tunja and features comfortable rooms, a spa, and a restaurant serving Colombian cuisine.

  • Hostal Casa de Lou: This hostel has a relaxed atmosphere and offers comfortable private rooms and dorms. It’s located close to the main attractions of the city.

  • Guesthouse Casa Verde: This guesthouse has a beautiful garden, a terrace with views of the city, and comfortable rooms. It’s located in a quiet residential area, just a few minutes from the city center.

What to Buy in Tunja: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

Visitors can find a variety of unique souvenirs and local products in Tunja, such as:

  • Woolen Crafts: Tunja is known for its woolen crafts, including blankets, ponchos, scarves, and hats. Visitors can find them in stores and markets throughout the city.

  • Pottery: The nearby town of Ráquira is famous for its pottery. Visitors can find handmade ceramic bowls, plates, and vases in a variety of colors and designs.

  • Coffee: Visitors can buy some of the best Colombian coffee at Hacienda Coloma or in local markets.

Local Culture and Customs of Tunja

Tunja has a rich cultural heritage, influenced by both indigenous and Spanish traditions. Visitors should be respectful of local customs, such as:

  • Religious Celebrations: The region is predominantly Catholic, and there are several religious celebrations throughout the year, such as Holy Week and the Feast of the Immaculate Conception.

  • Music and Dance: Tunja is known for its traditional music and dance, including bambuco, pasillo, and torbellino.

Best Time to Visit Tunja

The best time to visit Tunja is during the dry season, which runs from December to February and July to September. The weather is usually mild and pleasant, and there are fewer crowds.

Do’s and Don’ts of Tunja

Do:

  • Try the local cuisine and beverages.
  • Be respectful of local customs and traditions.
  • Explore the city on foot.
  • Learn some basic Spanish before your trip.

Don’t:

  • Wear revealing clothing in religious places.
  • Drink tap water.
  • Leave valuables unattended.

Safety and Health

Safety in Tunja

Tunja is generally a safe city for tourists. However, visitors should take common-sense precautions, such as:

  • Avoid walking alone at night.
  • Keep an eye on personal belongings in crowded places.
  • Use caution when using ATMs.

Health in Tunja

Visitors should consult a doctor or travel clinic before their trip to get up-to-date information on recommended vaccinations and medication. It is also advisable to bring insect repellent to protect against mosquito-borne diseases, such as dengue fever and malaria.

Getting to Tunja: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Tunja

The closest airport to Tunja is the Aeropuerto Internacional El Dorado in Bogotá, which is about 150 kilometers away. There are daily flights to Bogotá from major cities around the world.

Bus Stations Near Tunja

Tunja has two bus terminals: Terminal de Transportes de Tunja and Terminal del Norte. Both terminals offer routes to major cities in Colombia, including Bogotá, Medellín, and Barranquilla.

Other Options

Visitors can also rent a car to explore Tunja and the surrounding areas. Rental cars are available at the airport and in the city center. Taxis and public transportation are also available for short trips within the city.

How to Get Around in Tunja

Tunja is a compact city and is easily navigable on foot. Taxis and buses are also available for getting around the city. Visitors can also rent a bicycle to explore the city and its surroundings.

Where to Go Next from Tunja: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

There are many nearby destinations that visitors can explore from Tunja, including:

  • Villa de Leyva: This colonial town is a short drive from Tunja and is known for its cobbled streets, white-washed houses, and beautiful plaza.

  • Paipa: This small town is famous for its hot springs and offers visitors a variety of spa treatments and outdoor activities.

  • Sogamoso: This city is located about 20 kilometers from Tunja and is known for its pre-Columbian ruins and colonial architecture.

Conclusion

Tunja is a fascinating city that offers visitors a mix of history, culture, and outdoor adventures. Whether you’re interested in museums, natural attractions, or traditional cuisine, Tunja has something to offer everyone. With this comprehensive travel guide, you’ll be able to plan your trip and make the most of your visit.
