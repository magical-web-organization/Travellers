Introduction to Guiana Amazonian Park

Guiana Amazonian Park is a vast protected area in the heart of French Guiana, an overseas department of France in South America. Covering over 33,000 km2 (12,700 sq mi) of pristine rainforest and savannah, the park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a haven for biodiversity, featuring rare flora and fauna species, including jaguars, giant otters, harpy eagles, and anacondas.

The park is home to several indigenous communities, such as the Kali’na, Palikur, and Wayana, who have inhabited the region for centuries and still preserve their ancestral traditions and customs. Visitors can explore their villages, learn about their culture and lifestyle, and support their local economy by purchasing traditional handicrafts and souvenirs.

Local Culture and Customs of Guiana Amazonian Park

Visitors to Guiana Amazonian Park should be respectful of local customs and traditions. The indigenous communities have their own cultural practices, such as dances, songs, and rituals, and visitors should ask for permission before taking pictures or filming. It is also recommended to buy souvenirs directly from the locals rather than from street vendors or souvenir shops.

Best Time to Visit Guiana Amazonian Park

The best time to visit Guiana Amazonian Park is during the dry season, from June to November, when the weather is mild and there are fewer mosquitoes. The wet season, from December to May, can be challenging for hiking and outdoor activities as the trails can become muddy and slippery.

Safety and Health

Safety in Guiana Amazonian Park

Visitors to Guiana Amazonian Park should take precautions to ensure their safety, such as traveling in groups, avoiding isolated areas, and being aware of the risk of crime in some urban areas. It is recommended to hire a local guide for outdoor adventures, and to carry a reliable GPS device or map.

Health in Guiana Amazonian Park

Visitors to Guiana Amazonian Park should be up-to-date with their immunizations, including yellow fever, and should take precautions to avoid mosquito bites, as the region is endemic to mosquito-borne diseases such as dengue and malaria. It is also advisable to bring a water filtration device and to drink only bottled water or treated water.
