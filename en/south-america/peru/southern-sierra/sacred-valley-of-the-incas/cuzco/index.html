Introduction to Cuzco

Nestled in the Sacred Valley of the Incas in Southern Sierra, Peru rests the historic city of Cuzco. The former capital of the Inca Empire, Cuzco is rich with history and culture, making it an ideal destination for history buffs and adventure seekers. Today, it has become a bustling tourist hub and is well-known as the gateway to the world-famous Machu Picchu.

In this travel guide, we have compiled a comprehensive list of things to do, places to visit, and other helpful tips for making the most of your visit to Cuzco.

List of Places to Visit in Cuzco

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • Machu Picchu: One of the most famous UNESCO World Heritage sites, Machu Picchu is an ancient Incan city perched atop a mountain, accessible via a train ride from Cuzco.
  • Sacsayhuaman: An ancient Incan fortress located just outside the city with walls made from massive stones, some of which weigh up to 200 tons.
  • Coricancha: A former Incan temple, which was converted into a Christian church by Spanish conquerors. It’s the perfect spot to discover the mix of Inca and colonial architecture.
  • Cathedral of Santo Domingo: A stunning example of Colonial architecture located in the heart of the city with a spectacular collection of artworks.
  • San Pedro Market: A dynamic local market where you can haggle for souvenirs, clothes, and street food.

Natural Attractions of Cuzco: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • Rainbow Mountain: An incredible trekking adventure that takes you through stunning landscapes of the Andes and through the indigenous communities. You’ll be rewarded with breathtaking views of the famous Rainbow Mountain at the end.
  • Sacred Valley: Take a day trip to the Sacred Valley which is dotted with charming villages and ancient Inca ruins. You can also participate in many outdoor activities in the area, like mountain biking, horse riding, and river rafting.
  • Humantay Lake: A picturesque blue lake surrounded by snow-capped peaks makes for another great trekking opportunity.
  • Ausangate Glacier: A challenging trek that rewards you with stunning views of the mighty Ausangate glacier.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Cuzco

  • Inca Trail: A four-day trek through the Andes that concludes at the world-famous Machu Picchu.
  • Choquequirao Trek: This lesser-known trail journeys through the subtropical jungle, past Inca ruins and ends at the remote archaeological site of Choquequirao.
  • Maras Salt Mines Trek: A shorter and easier day trek that explores the salt mines of Maras, ancient Incan agricultural terraces, and ends up at the Inca agricultural site of Moray.

Best Things to Do in Cuzco: Activities and Tours

  • Cooking Classes: Learn to cook authentic Peruvian dishes like ceviche, delicious Salsas, and even the famous Peruvian Pisco Sour. The local markets are the perfect place to buy fresh ingredients.
  • Horseback riding: You can explore the countryside in a traditional way by horseback riding at an organic farm or through the Sacred Valley.
  • Guided tours: Local tour guides can offer you informative and holistic tours of Cuzco city and its surroundings explaining the history of the region.
  • Local music and dance shows: Enjoy a traditional song and dance performance, experiencing the local culture in a unique way.

Week-Long Itinerary for Cuzco

Day Activity
Day 1 Arrive in Cuzco and spend the day exploring the city center, enjoying a meal in San Pedro Market.
Day 2 Take a bumpy but beautiful drive to nearby Moray and explore the impressive Incan terraces. Afterward, head to the unique salt mines of Maras.
Day 3 Trek to the incredible Rainbow Mountain, taking in absolutely stunning views of the Andes Mountains along the way.
Day 4 Take a bus to Ollantaytambo and climb the ruins. Afterward, take the afternoon train to Aguas Calientes, the closest town to Machu Picchu.
Day 5 Explore Machu Picchu, taking a guided tour to learn about the history and significance of the UNESCO heritage site.
Day 6 Relax in the Sacred Valley, rafting down the river or enjoying a horseback riding excursion through the valley.
Day 7 Head back to Cuzco, wrap up your trip by exploring the city’s cultural attractions, local markets, and restaurants.

Food and Drink Scene of Cuzco

Must-Try Dishes

  • Ceviche: A refreshing and tangy seafood dish typically made with fresh raw fish “cooked” in lime juice and mixed with onion, garlic, and spices.
  • Lomo Saltado: A filling and flavorful dish that consists of marinated slices of steak, tomatoes, and onions served over a bed of French fries and rice.
  • Anticuchos: Skewered meats like chicken or beef hearts, marinated in spices and cooked over an open flame.

Local Beverages

  • Pisco Sour: A famous Peruvian cocktail, made with Pisco (a type of brandy) and lime juice, sweetened with syrup, and topped with egg white foam and bitters.
  • Chicha beer: A traditional, slightly fermented maize beer served in local taverns.

Where to Stay in Cuzco: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

  • Hotel Monasterio: A luxury hotel housed in a 16th-century Spanish colonial monastery, once used as a resting place for Incan nobility.
  • Casa San Blas Boutique Hotel: A charming and affordable boutique hotel located in the bohemian district of San Blas, with stunning views of the city from its rooftop.
  • Pariwana Hostel: One of the most popular backpacker hostels in the city known for its fun atmosphere and excellent amenities.

What to Buy in Cuzco: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

  • Alpaca wool clothing and textiles: From cozy sweaters to colorful blankets and rugs, Alpaca wool products make for a warm and unique souvenir.
  • Handmade pottery: Locally made pottery with intricate designs and vibrant colors is a popular souvenir among visitors.
  • Chocolate: Cuz’s Chocolate derives flavors and ingredients from the Amazon Rainforest and the Andean region. They are known for making quality chocolates.

Local Culture and Customs of Cuzco

  • Respect for the Pachamama: Pachamama, or Mother Earth, is a central figure in Incan culture and is still widely respected in Cuzco.
  • Bartering is expected: Haggling for prices is common practice in local markets and among street vendors, so don’t be afraid to negotiate prices.
  • Dress respectfully for cultural sites: When visiting religious sites, such as temples or churches, it’s important to dress respectfully, covering shoulders and knees.

Best Time to Visit Cuzco

The best time to visit Cuzco is from June to August, as this is the dry season and temperatures are mild. It’s also a busy time for tourism, so expect crowds and higher prices. The shoulder months of May, September, and October also offer good weather and fewer tourists.

Do’s and Don’ts of Cuzco

Do:

  • Respect the local customs: Learn about the local customs and traditions of Cuzco, and show respect for them.
  • Bring a hat and sunscreen: The sun can be strong in Cuzco, so be sure to bring protective gear.
  • Bring comfortable walking shoes: The city is full of hills and cobblestone streets, so comfortable shoes are a must.

Don’t:

  • Drink the tap water: It is best to stick to bottled water to avoid stomach upset.
  • Touch or climb on historical sites: To preserve the historical sites for future generations, it’s important to avoid touching or climbing on the ruins.

Safety and Health

Safety in Cuzco

Cuzco is considered a relatively safe city for tourists, but pickpocketing and theft can occur, so it’s important to stay vigilant and keep your belongings close at all times.

Health in Cuzco

Altitude sickness can be a real concern for visitors, as Cuzco sits at an altitude of 11,152 feet (3,399 meters). It’s important to take measures to acclimatize yourself to the altitude, including drinking plenty of water, taking it easy, avoiding alcohol, and chewing coca leaves.

Getting to Cuzco: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Cuzco

Alejandro Velasco Astete International Airport is the closest airport to Cuzco, located just 3 miles (5 kilometers) outside the city, and served by several airlines.

Bus Stations Near Cuzco

Cruz del Sur and Ormeño are both reputable bus companies with routes to and from Cuzco.

Other Options

Trains are also available for several destinations such as Machu Picchu and Puno.

How to Get Around in Cuzco

Many of the city’s attractions are within walking distance, but if you’re feeling tired or have further to go, you can take a taxi or a shared minivan.

Where to Go Next from Cuzco: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

  • Machu Picchu: Take a train from Cuzco to the world-famous Machu Picchu.
  • Puno and Lake Titicaca: A longer journey but worth it, Puno offers beautiful views of Lake Titicaca and cultural experiences of the Uros tribe.

Conclusion

Cuzco offers an experience like no other. A mix of ancient Inca culture and colonial history, visitors can find endless adventures, learn about the region’s history, and experience a rich cultural legacy. By following this guide, you’ll be prepared with everything you need to know to make your trip to Cuzco a memorable one.
