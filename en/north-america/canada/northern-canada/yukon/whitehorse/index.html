Introduction to Whitehorse

Whitehorse is the capital city of Yukon Territory, located in Northern Canada. Surrounded by breathtaking nature, this vibrant city offers visitors an immersive outdoor adventure experience. Whitehorse has a rich history and culture, which is evident in its many museums and art galleries. The city is also home to a thriving food and drink scene, making it the perfect destination for foodies.

If you’re planning a trip to Whitehorse, this comprehensive travel guide will help you make the most of your visit.

List of Places to Visit in Whitehorse

Whitehorse has something for everyone, whether you’re interested in history, culture, nature, or outdoor adventures. Here are some of the best places to visit in Whitehorse:

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • MacBride Museum of Yukon History: This museum features exhibits that showcase the human and natural history of Yukon. Visitors can explore the Gold Rush Gallery, the First Nations Gallery, and the Sam McGee Cabin.

  • Yukon Beringia Interpretive Centre: This center provides insight into the history of Beringia, a land bridge that once linked Asia and North America. The exhibitions include displays of fossils and prehistoric animals.

  • SS Klondike National Historic Site: This historic paddle-wheeler is a remnant of the Yukon’s Gold Rush era. The ship is docked on the Yukon River and visitors can explore its restored cabins and engine room.

  • Old Log Church Museum: The oldest building in Whitehorse, this museum explores the history of the early missionaries.

Natural Attractions of Whitehorse: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • Miles Canyon: Located just outside of Whitehorse, this narrow canyon carved out by the Yukon River is a popular spot for hiking and photography.

  • Takhini Hot Springs: Relax in natural hot springs while surrounded by the stunning mountain and forest scenery.

  • Kluane National Park and Reserve: This UNESCO World Heritage Site features the tallest peak in Canada and the largest ice field in the world outside of the poles, offering opportunities for hiking, climbing, and wildlife viewing.

  • Emerald Lake: A stunning turquoise-colored lake surrounded by the stunning scenery of the Kluane National Park.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Whitehorse

Whitehorse is a hiker’s paradise, with countless options for exploring the surrounding wilderness. Here are some of the best hiking and biking routes in the area:

  • The Millennium Trail: A walking and cycling trail that follows the Yukon River, offering beautiful river and mountain views.

  • Mt. McIntyre Recreation Trails: A network of hiking, mountain biking, and Nordic skiing trails that offer beautiful views of the city and the surrounding mountains.

  • Grey Mountain: A challenging hike for experienced hikers. At the top, you will be rewarded with stunning panoramic views of the surrounding mountains and valleys.

Best Things to Do in Whitehorse: Activities and Tours

Whether you prefer to explore on your own or partake in a guided tour, there are plenty of activities to choose from in Whitehorse:

  • Northern Lights Viewing: Whitehorse is one of the best places in the world to see the Northern Lights. Professional tour companies offer tours that take you to the best viewing locations.

  • Dog Sledding: Experience the thrill of dog sledding on a guided tour through the scenic wilderness.

  • Canoeing and Kayaking: Whitehorse has many lakes and rivers that offer the chance for visitors to take guided or self-guided tours for a unique outdoor experience.

  • Tooley’s Historic Site: Tooley’s offers visitors a chance to explore an abandoned mining town with original buildings, church, and schoolhouse from the gold rush era.

Week-Long Itinerary for Whitehorse

Day 1: MacBride Museum of Yukon History and a scenic walk along the Yukon River. Day 2: Takhini Hot Springs and an evening Northern Lights Tour. Day 3: Guided tour and hiking in Kluane National Park. Day 4: Dogsledding adventure and a visit to SS Klondike National Historic Site. Day 5: Canoeing and kayaking tour and exploring the city on the Millennium Trail. Day 6: Grey Mountain Hike and enjoying the white water rapids on the Tutshi River. Day 7: Tooley’s Historic Site visit and an evening cocktails at the Aurora Sky bar watching the Northern Lights.

Food and Drink Scene of Whitehorse

Whitehorse has a thriving food and drink scene that is influenced by the region’s history and culture. Here are some of the must-try dishes and local beverages:

Must-Try Dishes

  • Bannock: A bread-like food that was a staple of the Indigenous diet and gold rush era.

  • Wild Game: Yukon is known for its abundance of wild game such as caribou, arctic char, and moose, which can be found on many menus in the city.

  • Sourdough Pancakes: Another Gold Rush-era staple, topped with real Yukon maple syrup.

Local Beverages

  • Yukon Brewing: The well-known craft brewery has a tasting room where visitors can try the brewery’s range of beer.

  • Aurora Brewing Company: Newer brewery featuring IPA’s, lagers, and pilsners refreshing brews.

  • Birch Sap Wine: This drink is produced from birch sap, a traditional Indigenous beverage.

Where to Stay in Whitehorse: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

  • Westmark Whitehorse Hotel & Conference Centre: A downtown hotel with spacious rooms and on-site dining.

  • Casa Loma: A comfortable bed and breakfast with mountain views and skiing nearby.

  • Hi-Whitehorse Hostel: A budget-friendly hostel with a communal kitchen, laundry, and shared living spaces, located in the heart of downtown.

  • Midnight Sun Inn Bed and Breakfast: Cozy cabins and rooms on the outskirts of town.

What to Buy in Whitehorse: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

  • First Nations Art: Local galleries and studios feature artwork that reflects the region’s Indigenous cultures, including carvings, beadwork, and paintings.

  • Yukon Gold: Yukon is known for its gold mining industry and visitors can purchase gold nuggets or gold jewelry.

  • Birch Syrup: Made from birch sap, this unique product is a local delicacy.

Local Culture and Customs of Whitehorse

The local culture of Whitehorse is shaped by Yukon’s Indigenous peoples who maintain rich traditions and histories. Visitors should be respectful of these customs and traditions, for example, dress appropriately for cultural events, and ask permission before taking photos.

Best Time to Visit Whitehorse

Whitehorse has a continental subarctic climate with colder winters, lasting from October to April, and warmer summers, between May and September.

The best time to visit and experience Northern Lights is during the fall and winter months (October to March). Summer months are perfect for hiking and outdoor adventures and also for attending the many summer festivals in the city.

Do’s and Don’ts of Whitehorse

Do:

  • Respect Indigenous culture and traditions.
  • Observe wildlife from a safe distance.
  • Carry bear spray while hiking.
  • Respect the natural environment by properly disposing of trash and using designated camping areas.

Don’t:

  • Approach wildlife or feed them.
  • Smoke in public spaces.
  • Litter or disturb the natural environment.

Safety and Health

Safety in Whitehorse

Whitehorse is a very safe city, with low crime rates, but visitors should exercise precautions when exploring the wilderness. Always follow safety guidelines, especially if you plan for an outdoor adventure alone.

Health in Whitehorse

It is important to get health insurance before your travels! The Yukon government provides emergency services, but any other emergency services cost high. So, have insurance in-case of an emergency.

Getting to Whitehorse: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Whitehorse

  • Erik Nielsen Whitehorse International Airport: The city is served by this international airport, which has daily direct flights from Vancouver, Calgary, and Edmonton, and seasonally from Frankfurt.

Bus Stations Near Whitehorse

  • Greyhound Bus Lines: There are several bus stations throughout Whitehorse.

Other Options

  • Driving is also an option for those that prefer having transportation for their visit to Whitehorse.

How to Get Around in Whitehorse

For those who prefer to explore the city on foot, the center of the Whitehorse is easily walkable. Renting a car is recommended for exploring the surrounding area or, if you’re going to be in the city for longer, cycling and kayaking options are reasonable.

Where to Go Next from Whitehorse: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

  • Dawson City: This historic town is a four-hour drive from Whitehorse and is home to well-preserved Gold Rush-era architecture.

  • Tombstone Territorial Park: A vast wilderness area featuring jagged peaks and wide-open valleys, unique landscapes, and hiking trails to keep you busy and also many opportunities for aurora viewing.

  • Haines Junction: A small town located at the entrance of Kluane National Park, home to the magnificent park gateway and visitor centre.

Conclusion

Whitehorse, Yukon, is truly a unique destination in Northern Canada. By following this comprehensive travel guide, you will be able to make the most out of your experience in this vibrant, wilderness-filled city. With its rich history and culture, stunning outdoor adventures, and thriving food and drink scene, it offers something for every traveler. Plan your visit for your next vacation. Happy travelling!
