Introduction to Deep East Texas

Deep East Texas is a region that covers 12 counties in the easternmost part of the state of Texas. Known for its dense pine forests, rolling hills, and abundant lakes and rivers, this area is a haven for outdoor enthusiasts. Deep East Texas also boasts a rich history and culture, with museums and historical landmarks scattered throughout the region.

List of Places to Visit in Deep East Texas

Whether you’re interested in exploring the outdoors, learning about the region’s history and culture, or simply relaxing in a peaceful setting, Deep East Texas has something for everyone. Here are some of the top places to visit:

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • Museum of East Texas: This museum in Lufkin features exhibits on local history, art, and culture, including a collection of antique firearms.
  • Nacogdoches Historic Sites: Nacogdoches is the oldest town in Texas, and is home to a variety of historical sites and landmarks, including the Sterne-Hoya House Museum and Library, the Old Stone Fort Museum, and the Durst-Taylor Historic House and Gardens.
  • Texas Forestry Museum: Located in Lufkin, this museum showcases the history and importance of the forestry industry in Texas, with interactive exhibits and displays.
  • Caddo Mounds State Historic Site: This site near Alto includes a museum and three Native American mounds, which are believed to date back to 800 AD.

Natural Attractions of Deep East Texas: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • Big Thicket National Preserve: This 100,000-acre preserve includes hiking trails, canoeing and kayaking opportunities, and diverse wildlife, including alligators, armadillos, and over 200 species of birds.
  • Toledo Bend Reservoir: This massive lake straddles the Texas-Louisiana border and is a popular destination for fishing, boating, and water sports.
  • Davy Crockett National Forest: This 160,000-acre forest offers camping, hiking, fishing, and hunting, as well as scenic drives and opportunities to see wildlife.
  • Sabine National Wildlife Refuge: Located in the southern part of Deep East Texas, this refuge is a prime spot for birdwatching, as well as fishing and hunting.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Deep East Texas

Deep East Texas is a paradise for outdoor enthusiasts, with an abundance of hiking and biking trails that showcase the region’s natural beauty. Here are a few of the best trails to explore:

  • Neches Bluff Overlook Trail: This 6-mile trail in Davy Crockett National Forest offers stunning views of the Neches River and nearby bluffs.
  • Martin Dies Jr. State Park Trail System: This park near Jasper offers over 10 miles of hiking trails, ranging from easy to difficult, as well as a paved biking trail.
  • Kountze Trail System: This system of trails in the Big Thicket National Preserve includes over 20 miles of hiking and biking trails, with options for all skill levels.

Best Things to Do in Deep East Texas: Activities and Tours

In addition to hiking, biking, and exploring the outdoors, there are plenty of other activities and tours to enjoy in Deep East Texas. Here are some of the best:

  • Pineywoods Native Plant Center: This center in Nacogdoches showcases the diverse flora of the region, with walking tours and educational programs.
  • Angelina County Farmers Market: This weekly market in Lufkin offers locally grown produce, handmade crafts, and live music.
  • Dining and Drinking Tours: Discover the unique cuisine and local beverages of Deep East Texas with a guided tour, such as the Pineywoods Wine Trail or the Texas Pines and Prairies Trail.

Week-Long Itinerary for Deep East Texas

If you have a full week to explore Deep East Texas, here’s a suggested itinerary:

  • Day 1: Tour the Museum of East Texas and explore Lufkin.
  • Day 2: Hike the Neches Bluff Overlook Trail and camp in Davy Crockett National Forest.
  • Day 3: Visit the Caddo Mounds State Historic Site and continue on to Toledo Bend Reservoir for fishing and watersports.
  • Day 4: Take a guided food and drink tour of the region, stopping at local wineries and breweries.
  • Day 5: Spend the day exploring Nacogdoches Historic Sites, including the Old Stone Fort Museum and Library.
  • Day 6: Visit the Texas Forestry Museum and hike the Kountze Trail System in the Big Thicket National Preserve.
  • Day 7: Relax and unwind at one of the region’s many lakeside cabins or vacation rentals.

Food and Drink Scene of Deep East Texas

Deep East Texas has a rich culinary scene, with a focus on fresh, locally sourced ingredients. Here are some must-try dishes and local beverages:

Must-Try Dishes

  • Fried catfish: A regional specialty, often served with hushpuppies and coleslaw.
  • Pineywoods gumbo: A hearty stew made with chicken, sausage, and okra.
  • Texas-style barbecue: Slow-cooked beef brisket, ribs, and sausage, served with sides like baked beans and potato salad.

Local Beverages

  • Lufkin Coca-Cola Bottling Company: Take a tour of this historic bottling plant, which still produces classic Coke in glass bottles.
  • Pineywoods Winery: Sample the award-winning wines made from grapes grown right in Deep East Texas.
  • Jasper County Brewing Company: A family-owned brewery that specializes in small-batch, handcrafted beers.

Where to Stay in Deep East Texas: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Whether you prefer a rustic cabin in the woods or a luxurious hotel in the city, Deep East Texas has plenty of options for lodging. Here are a few recommendations:

  • M&M Ranch House: This guesthouse in Zavalla offers private rooms and shared spaces, with access to hiking, fishing, and other outdoor activities.
  • Courtyard by Marriott Lufkin: A modern hotel in the heart of Lufkin, with a pool, fitness center, and on-site restaurant.
  • Caddo Lake Lodge: This cozy bed and breakfast near Uncertain offers views of the lake and access to canoeing and kayaking.

What to Buy in Deep East Texas: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

If you’re looking for unique souvenirs and gifts, Deep East Texas has plenty of options. Here are a few local products to bring home:

  • Pineywoods Honey: Locally harvested and bottled honey, available at farmers markets and specialty stores throughout the region.
  • Handmade crafts and artwork: From pottery to paintings, there are plenty of talented local artists to discover in Deep East Texas.
  • Outdoor gear and fishing equipment: Stock up on gear for your next hiking or fishing trip at one of the region’s many outdoor outfitters.

Local Culture and Customs of Deep East Texas

Deep East Texas is known for its friendly, welcoming locals and rich cultural heritage. Here are a few customs and traditions to be aware of:

  • Southern hospitality: People in Deep East Texas are known for their warmth and hospitality, so don’t be surprised if strangers strike up a conversation or offer a helping hand.
  • Country music: From honky-tonk bars to outdoor festivals, country music is a big part of the culture in Deep East Texas.
  • Outdoor traditions: Hunting, fishing, and other outdoor activities are a way of life for many people in Deep East Texas, and are an important part of the region’s culture and traditions.

Best Time to Visit Deep East Texas

The best time to visit Deep East Texas depends on your interests and preferences. Spring and fall offer mild temperatures and beautiful foliage, making them ideal for outdoor activities and scenic drives. Summer can be hot and humid, but it’s also a popular time for fishing, boating, and swimming in the region’s lakes and rivers. Winter is the quietest season in Deep East Texas, with fewer tourists and a more laid-back vibe.

Do’s and Don’ts of Deep East Texas

Here are a few tips for making the most of your trip to Deep East Texas:

Do:

  • Explore the outdoors: With so much natural beauty to discover, hiking, fishing, and camping are must-do activities in Deep East Texas.
  • Try the local cuisine: From barbecue to catfish, Deep East Texas has a rich culinary scene that’s worth exploring.
  • Connect with the locals: Whether it’s striking up a conversation at a farmers market or chatting with a bartender at a local watering hole, getting to know the locals can be a highlight of your trip.

Don’t:

  • Underestimate the heat: Summer temperatures in Deep East Texas can be brutal, so be sure to stay hydrated and take breaks in the shade.
  • Overlook the history and culture: From museums to historical sites, there’s a wealth of history and culture to explore in Deep East Texas.
  • Forget the bug spray: Lush forests and waterways mean plenty of mosquitoes and other insects, so come prepared with insect repellent.

Safety and Health

Here are a few things to keep in mind when it comes to safety and health in Deep East Texas:

Safety in Deep East Texas

  • Be aware of wildlife: From alligators to snakes, there are plenty of potentially dangerous animals in the region’s forests and waterways. Stay alert and give wildlife plenty of space.
  • Watch the weather: Deep East Texas is prone to occasional severe weather, including thunderstorms and tornadoes. Be sure to check the forecast and be prepared if conditions turn dangerous.
  • Stay hydrated: The heat and humidity in Deep East Texas can be intense, so be sure to drink plenty of water and take breaks in cooler areas.

Health in Deep East Texas

  • Beware of tick-borne illnesses: Lyme disease and other tick-borne illnesses are a risk in Deep East Texas, so be sure to wear long pants and sleeves when hiking, and check yourself for ticks frequently.
  • Stay safe on the water: Lakes and rivers can be dangerous, especially for inexperienced swimmers or boaters. Follow all safety guidelines and wear a life jacket when boating or swimming.
  • Practice sun safety: The sun can be strong in Deep East Texas, so be sure to wear sunscreen and a hat, and take breaks in the shade as needed.

Getting to Deep East Texas: Transportation Options and Logistics

Deep East Texas is accessible by car, bus, and plane. Here are some of the options for getting to the region:

Airports Near Deep East Texas

  • George Bush Intercontinental Airport in Houston, Texas (approximately 125 miles from Deep East Texas).
  • Shreveport Regional Airport in Shreveport, Louisiana (approximately 85 miles from Deep East Texas).

Bus Stations Near Deep East Texas

  • Greyhound operates several bus stations throughout the region, including in Lufkin, Jasper, and Nacogdoches.

Other Options

  • Driving: Deep East Texas is easily accessible by car, with major highways including Interstate 45 and US Highways 59 and 190.
  • RVs and Campers: There are numerous RV parks and campgrounds throughout Deep East Texas, offering a variety of amenities and services.
  • Rental Cars: Rental car companies such as Enterprise and Hertz operate in Deep East Texas.

How to Get Around in Deep East Texas

The best way to get around Deep East Texas is by car, as public transportation options are limited. Rental cars are available at major airports and in the larger cities, and there are also several taxi and ride-sharing services available.

Where to Go Next from Deep East Texas: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

If you have more time to explore the region, there are several nearby destinations that are worth a visit. Here are a few ideas:

  • Houston: The largest city in Texas is only a few hours’ drive from Deep East Texas, and offers a wealth of cultural attractions, shopping, and dining.
  • New Orleans: This Louisiana city is known for its vibrant music scene, unique architecture, and world-famous cuisine, and is approximately a 4-hour drive from Deep East Texas.
  • Texas Gulf Coast: The Gulf of Mexico is only a few hours’ drive from Deep East Texas, with popular destinations including Galveston, Corpus Christi, and South Padre Island.

Conclusion

Deep East Texas is a region that truly has something for everyone, from outdoor adventures to rich culture and history to delicious cuisine. Whether you’re planning a week-long vacation or just a weekend getaway, this comprehensive guide will help you make the most of your trip. So pack your bags and get ready to explore the natural beauty and hospitality of Deep East Texas.
