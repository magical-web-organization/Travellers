Introduction to Gates of the Arctic National Park

Gates of the Arctic National Park is located in Arctic Alaska, in the United States of America. It is one of the most isolated and remote national parks in the USA and is only accessible by foot, plane or dogsled. Despite being one of the least visited national parks in the country, Gates of the Arctic offers an incredible wilderness experience with untouched landscapes and a unique opportunity to connect with nature.

List of Places to Visit in Gates of the Arctic National Park

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • The Inupiat Heritage Center: This center showcases the culture of the Inupiat people and their traditional way of life, including hunting, subsistence, and unique crafts.
  • The Nunamiut Heritage Center: Located in Anaktuvuk Pass, this center honors the Nunamiut people, their culture, and way of life. Visitors can learn about traditional subsistence practices and artistic expressions.

Natural Attractions of Gates of the Arctic National Park: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • Brooks Range: As the northernmost mountain range in North America, Brooks range offers hikers and mountaineers stunning views and diverse wildlife.
  • Arctic Circle: The park is home to the Arctic Circle, an imaginary line located at 66 degrees north of the equator. Take a flightseeing tour to see breathtaking views of the landscape, especially during the summer months when the sun never sets.
  • Aiyagomahala Valley: Hike through the scenic Aiyagomahala Valley for some of the best views of the park and its untouched wilderness.
  • Anaktuvuk Pass: Situated in the heart of the Brooks Range, this village is one of the few areas in the park that is home to the indigenous Arctic people. Visitors can gain insight into the traditional way of life of the Nunamiut people.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Gates of the Arctic National Park

Gates of the Arctic offers an extensive network of hiking trails and backcountry routes. Popular hikes include the Arrigetch Peaks, the Gates of the Arctic Traverse and the Itkillik Brooks Range Traverse. Biking is not allowed in the park, however, fat-tire cycling is a popular activity during winter months.

Best Things to Do in Gates of the Arctic National Park: Activities and Tours

  • Flightseeing: Take a scenic flight tour to explore the park’s untouched wilderness and spectacular landscapes. Most tours fly over the neighboring Arctic National Wildlife Refuge as well.
  • Dog Sledding: Experience Alaska’s state sport with a dog-sledding tour. Learn how to mush a team of dogs across the snowy landscape of the park.
  • Rafting and Kayaking: The rivers of the park offer a unique opportunity to explore the park’s wildlife and scenic beauty while navigating the waterways.
  • Backcountry Camping: Gates of the Arctic National Park is the perfect destination for those who enjoy camping in remote areas. Follow the Leave No Trace principles to minimize your impact on the environment.

Week-Long Itinerary for Gates of the Arctic National Park

Day 1-2: Fly into Fairbanks and spend a couple of days exploring the city’s museums, cultural centers, and local restaurants.

Day 3: Take a scenic flight to the park and hike around the surrounding areas.

Day 4-6: Go on a multi-day backpacking trip and camp at one of the backcountry campsites.

Day 7: Return to Fairbanks and depart for your next destination.

Food and Drink Scene of Gates of the Arctic National Park

Must-Try Dishes

  • Caribou: A staple food of the indigenous people of Alaska and a local delicacy.
  • Eskimo Ice Cream: A traditional treat made with a combination of berries and animal fat.
  • Salmon: Alaskan salmon is famous for its flavor and nutritional value. Try it smoked or grilled.

Local Beverages

  • Birch syrup: A sweet and flavorful syrup made from the sap of birch trees.
  • Alaskan beer: Enjoy a fresh, locally-brewed beer after a long day of hiking and exploring.

Where to Stay in Gates of the Arctic National Park: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

There are no hotels or hostels in Gates of the Arctic National Park. Visitors can stay in nearby villages or choose to camp in the park’s backcountry campsites.

What to Buy in Gates of the Arctic National Park: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

  • Traditional Crafts: Indigenous Alaskans are known for their unique jewelry, instruments, and carvings. Look for these one-of-a-kind items in local shops.
  • Artisanal Foods: Bring home a taste of Alaska with locally sourced food products, including birch syrup, smoked salmon, and locally sourced honey.

Local Culture and Customs of Gates of the Arctic National Park

The indigenous people of Arctic Alaska have a rich cultural history stretching back thousands of years. Respect the local customs and traditions, including honoring elders and participating in community events.

Best Time to Visit Gates of the Arctic National Park

The best time to visit Gates of the Arctic National Park is during the summer months of June, July, and August when the weather is mild and the sun never sets. Be prepared for unpredictable weather conditions year-round.

Do’s and Don’ts of Gates of the Arctic National Park

Do:

  • Respect the local indigenous people and their traditions.
  • Follow the Leave No Trace principles when camping and hiking.
  • Dress appropriately for the weather and the activity.

Don’t:

  • Deviate from marked trails without a professional guide.
  • Leave trash or waste in the park or surrounding areas.
  • Disturb or feed local wildlife.

Safety and Health

Safety in Gates of the Arctic National Park

  • Be aware of the danger of hypothermia and frostbite in winter months.
  • Carry bear spray while hiking and camping.
  • Consult with an experienced guide before attempting any backcountry activities.

Health in Gates of the Arctic National Park

  • The park is quite remote, so it is important to bring enough medication and any necessary medical equipment.
  • Be prepared for the altitude of the area, as the park ranges from 1,200 to 8,800 feet above sea level.

Getting to Gates of the Arctic National Park: Transportation Options and Logistics

The park is only accessible by foot, plane, or dogsled. Visitors can fly into Fairbanks or Bettles and arrange a connecting flight or charter a private plane to the park.

Airports Near Gates of the Arctic National Park

  • Fairbanks International Airport
  • Bettles Airport

Bus Stations Near Gates of the Arctic National Park

  • There are no bus stations in Gates of the Arctic National Park.

Other Options

  • Charter a private plane or helicopter to get to the park.

How to Get Around in Gates of the Arctic National Park

The best way to get around in Gates of the Arctic National Park is by hiking or dogsledding. Visitors should respect the park’s pristine environment and practice Leave No Trace principles.

Where to Go Next from Gates of the Arctic National Park: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

  • Arctic National Wildlife Refuge: Take a trip to explore the wildlife and natural beauty of the neighboring national wildlife refuge.
  • Prudhoe Bay: A hub of the North Slope Oil Fields, visitors can take a tour and learn about the state’s oil industry.

Conclusion

Gates of the Arctic National Park is a unique and remote wilderness experience for those looking for an adventure beyond the typical tourist destinations. Plan ahead, respect the environment, and make sure to book travel and accommodations well in advance.
