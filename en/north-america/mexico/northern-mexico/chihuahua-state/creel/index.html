Introduction to Creel

Creel is a picturesque town located in the Sierra Madre Occidental range in the state of Chihuahua, Mexico. It’s a popular destination for nature aficionados, adventure seekers, and culture buffs, thanks to its stunning natural landscapes, diverse wildlife, rich indigenous heritage, and welcoming locals. With a population of around 6,000, Creel is a small yet vibrant town that has managed to preserve its traditional architecture, customs, and traditions, making it a perfect place to immerse oneself in Northern Mexican culture. In this guide, we will provide you with all the information you need to plan your trip to Creel and make the most of your time there.

List of Places to Visit in Creel

Creel abounds with both natural and cultural attractions that will keep you entertained and amazed throughout your stay. Here are some of the must-visit places in Creel:

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • Museum of the Missionary Sisters: A museum that chronicles the founding of Creel and its development through the work of Catholic missionaries.
  • Casa de las Artesanías: A colorful building that showcases the handmade crafts of the local indigenous tribes, including baskets, pottery, textiles, and hand-carved wooden figurines.
  • San Ignacio Mission: A beautiful Jesuit mission church built in the 17th century, with a rich history and impressive architecture.
  • The valley of the mushrooms: A geological formation of giant rocks that resemble mushrooms, where visitors can hike and admire the unique landscape.

Natural Attractions of Creel: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • Copper Canyon: A UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most striking natural wonders of Mexico, consisting of a series of canyons that are deeper and wider than the Grand Canyon.
  • Lake Arareco: A serene lake surrounded by pine forests that offers kayaking, fishing, and magnificent views of the surrounding mountains.
  • Cusárare Waterfalls: A set of cascading waterfalls that drop over 30 meters and are located in a scenic canyon with hiking trails and picnic areas.
  • Valle de los Monjes: A fascinating rock formation that resembles a group of monks in prayer, with hiking trails that lead to panoramic views of the valley.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Creel

Creel is a paradise for hiking and biking enthusiasts, with a variety of trails that cater to all levels of experience. Some of the most popular routes are:

  1. Rarámuri Path: A rugged and challenging trail that runs through the Copper Canyon and rewards hikers with stunning views of the canyons, rivers, and waterfalls.
  2. Valley of the Frogs: A moderate trail that traverses a forested valley and leads to a hidden waterfall and a pond inhabited by frogs.
  3. Mirador Piedra Volada: An easy trail that leads to a magnificent viewpoint where visitors can see the waterfall of the same name, the valley of the mushrooms, and the Copper Canyon.

Best Things to Do in Creel: Activities and Tours

Aside from hiking and biking, Creel offers a variety of activities and tours that will keep visitors engaged and entertained. Some of the best things to do in Creel are:

  1. Take a train ride through the Copper Canyon: The Chihuahua al Pacífico (ChePe) railway is one of the most scenic train rides in the world, taking riders through tunnels, bridges, and towering cliffs while offering breathtaking views of the canyons.
  2. Go horseback riding: Horseback riding is a popular way to explore the countryside of Creel, with many ranches offering guided rides through the mountains.
  3. Visit a Rarámuri community: The Rarámuri people, also known as Tarahumara, are the indigenous inhabitants of the Copper Canyon region, and visiting their communities is a great way to learn about their way of life, history, and traditions.

Week-Long Itinerary for Creel

Here’s a suggested itinerary for a one week stay in Creel:

Day 1: Arrive in Creel and explore the town center, including the Museum of the Missionary Sisters, the Casa de las Artesanías, and the San Ignacio Mission. Day 2: Take a train ride through the Copper Canyon and stop at Divisadero, where you can admire the breathtaking views and visit the souvenir market. Day 3: Hike the Rarámuri Path and witness the stunning waterfalls and landscapes of the Copper Canyon. Day 4: Go horseback riding through the pine forests and valleys surrounding Creel. Day 5: Visit the Cusárare Waterfalls and the Valle de los Monjes, followed by a relaxing afternoon at Lake Arareco. Day 6: Attend a cultural show or join a workshop to learn about the Rarámuri traditions and crafts. Day 7: Enjoy a leisurely morning exploring more of Creel’s sights, then depart for your next destination.

Food and Drink Scene of Creel

Creel’s cuisine is a fusion of indigenous and Spanish flavors, with a focus on grilled meats, hearty stews, and handmade tortillas. Here are some must-try dishes and beverages in Creel:

Must-Try Dishes

  • Asado de venado: Roasted venison seasoned with aromatic spices and chilies, served with rice and beans.
  • Gorditas de harina: Small, thick corn tortillas stuffed with refried beans, cheese, and meat.
  • Caldo de oso: A hearty beef soup with pieces of vegetables and chunks of beef with the bone still in.

Local Beverages

  • Sotol: A distilled liquor made from the agave plant, which has a smoky, earthy flavor.
  • Agua de vainilla: A sweet vanilla-infused water that is refreshing and thirst-quenching.
  • Tepache: A homemade fermented drink made from pineapple and spices that is tangy and slightly fizzy.

Where to Stay in Creel: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Creel has a range of accommodation options to suit all budgets and preferences. Here are some of the best places to stay in Creel:

  • Parador Santa Cruz: A charming guesthouse with cozy rooms, a restaurant serving authentic local cuisine, and friendly staff.
  • Hotel Mirador: A modern, comfortable hotel with spacious rooms, a heated indoor pool, and stunning views of the surrounding mountains.
  • Casa Margarita: A budget-friendly hostel with clean, simple rooms, a communal kitchen, and a central location near the town square.

What to Buy in Creel: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

Creel is renowned for its handicrafts, produced by the local Rarámuri people using traditional techniques and materials. Here are some unique souvenirs and local products to bring home from Creel:

  • Wooden carvings: Intricately carved wooden figures of animals, people, and natural scenes, made by skilled Rarámuri artisans.
  • Textiles: Colorful, woven blankets, rugs, and shawls, made from wool and cotton dyed with natural pigments.
  • Baskets: Sturdy, handwoven baskets made from local grasses and reeds, ranging from small trinket boxes to large laundry hampers.

Local Culture and Customs of Creel

The Rarámuri people have a rich, distinctive culture that is deeply intertwined with the natural world. They are known for their long-distance running prowess, as well as their live music, colorful costumes, and intricate crafts. Visitors to Creel should be respectful of Rarámuri customs and traditions, such as seeking permission before taking photographs or entering their homes, and avoiding certain areas during religious ceremonies.

Best Time to Visit Creel

The best time to visit Creel depends on your preferred activities and weather preferences. Summer (June-August) is the peak tourist season, with warm temperatures and frequent rain showers. Fall (September-November) offers crisp, cool weather and stunning fall foliage, while winter (December-February) can be chilly and snowy, but offers the opportunity to see the landscapes covered in white. Spring (March-May) is the driest season with mild temperatures, fewer crowds, and a chance to see wildflowers and newborn animals.

Do’s and Don’ts of Creel

Do:

  • Try the local cuisine and beverages, including sotol, gorditas, and caldo de oso.
  • Learn some phrases in Spanish and Tarahumara to communicate with the locals.
  • Respect the natural environment and follow the Leave No Trace principles when hiking and camping.
  • Wear comfortable, sturdy and appropriate shoes and clothing for outdoor activities and bring sunscreen, a hat, and a jacket.

Don’t:

  • Take photos of the Rarámuri people or their homes without permission.
  • Buy souvenirs made from endangered or protected species, such as feathers, furs, or seashells.
  • Litter or disturb the wildlife and ecosystems of the Copper Canyon.
  • Underestimate the altitude and extreme weather changes.

Safety and Health

Safety in Creel

Creel is a safe and welcoming town, but visitors should exercise common sense precautions to avoid any incidents. Avoid leaving valuables unattended in public places and stay alert when walking around at night. Also, while visiting indigenous communities and buying handicrafts from them is a great way to support the local economy, be aware of scams and do some research on reputable cooperatives beforehand. Finally, it’s important to follow safety rules and guidelines for outdoor activities like hiking and biking on remote trails.

Health in Creel

Creel is at a high altitude, with the elevation ranging from 7,800 to 8,200 feet above sea level. Visitors may experience altitude sickness, such as headaches, dizziness, nausea, or shortness of breath. It’s recommended to take it easy for the first few days, drink plenty of water, avoid alcohol and high-fat foods, and take medication if necessary. In terms of other health concerns, Creel has basic medical facilities and pharmacies, but it’s recommended to bring any necessary prescription medication and to purchase travel insurance before arriving.

Getting to Creel: Transportation Options and Logistics

Creel is located in Chihuahua, about 170 miles southwest of the city of Chihuahua. Here are some ways to get to Creel:

Airports Near Creel

The closest airport is General Roberto Fierro Villalobos International Airport in Chihuahua City, which has flights from major cities in Mexico and the US. From the airport, visitors can take a taxi or a bus to the Central Bus Station.

Bus Stations Near Creel

The Central Bus Station in Chihuahua City has regular buses to Creel, with a travel time of around 6 hours. Several bus companies operate on this route, including Omnibus de México, Turimex Internacional, and ETN.

Other Options

Visitors can also drive to Creel, either by renting a car or by hiring a private driver. The drive from Chihuahua to Creel takes about 4 hours and follows a scenic route.

How to Get Around in Creel

Creel is a small town that can be easily explored on foot, but visitors who want to venture outside the town limits or explore the Copper Canyon region should consider renting a car or hiring a driver. Taxis are also available in Creel, but negotiate the price beforehand or ensure that the cab has a working meter.

Where to Go Next from Creel: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

Creel is a good starting point to explore the Copper Canyon region, one of the most impressive natural wonders of North America. From Creel, visitors can take a train or a bus to other towns and attractions in the region, such as:

  • Batopilas: A historic silver mining town at the bottom of the canyon, accessible only by a challenging dirt road.
  • Urique: A small town on the banks of the Urique River, known for its spectacular views and outdoor activities.
  • Basaseachi National Park: A protected area that features the Basaseachi Waterfall, one of the tallest in Mexico, and a rich biodiversity of flora and fauna.

Conclusion

Creel is a truly unique travel destination that combines stunning natural landscapes with rich cultural traditions, offering something for every type of traveler. With this comprehensive guide, you’ll have all the information you need to plan your trip to Creel, from the best places to visit and things to do, to tips for staying safe and healthy. Get ready for an unforgettable adventure in Northern Mexico!
