Introduction to Cumbria

Located in North West England, Cumbria is a picturesque and historic county, home to the Lake District National Park, stunning scenery, and quaint towns and villages. Famous for its natural beauty, Cumbria is a must-visit destination for those who love the great outdoors.

List of Places to Visit in Cumbria

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

Cumbria is steeped in history and culture, making it an excellent destination for art and history lovers alike. Here are some of the top cultural attractions to visit:

  • Carlisle Castle: A medieval fortress built in the 12th century, offering stunning views of the city.

  • The Wordsworth Museum and Dove Cottage: The former home of poet William Wordsworth, now a museum dedicated to his life and works.

  • Tullie House Museum & Art Gallery: A great place for art and history lovers, with galleries showcasing artwork from pre-Roman times to the present day.

  • St. Bees Priory: A stunning 12th-century church that has been a place of worship for over 900 years.

Natural Attractions of Cumbria: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

Cumbria is widely known for its stunning natural landscapes; it boasts many natural attractions that create the perfect backdrop for outdoor activities. Here are some of the best natural attractions to explore:

  • Lake District National Park: With its stunning lakes, forests and mountains, the Lake District National Park offers endless opportunities for outdoor adventure.

  • Hadrian’s Wall: An iconic Roman wall that spans 73 miles across England, with several forts and towns along the way.

  • Scafell Pike: England’s highest mountain at 978 meters, offering stunning views of the Lake District and beyond.

  • Derwentwater: A scenic lake that offers plenty of outdoor activities such as kayaking, and is surrounded by picturesque towns and villages.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Cumbria

Cumbria is a paradise for hikers and bikers. Here are some of the best hiking/biking routes that you can explore in the region:

  • Helvellyn: This mountain standing at 950 meters is a popular spot for experienced hikers.

  • Catbells: A relatively easy and family-friendly hike that offers stunning views of the lake district.

  • The C2C (Coast to Coast) Cycle Route: A popular 140-mile cycling route that traverses the region from the Irish Sea to the North Sea along scenic country lanes and through historic towns.

Best Things to Do in Cumbria: Activities and Tours

Beyond hiking and biking, there are various activities to enjoy in Cumbria. Here are some exciting things to do in the county:

  • Visit a Farm: Cumbria is known for its farms, with many authentic working farms that offer tours and provide opportunities to learn about farming and animal husbandry.

  • Take a Boat Trip: With several scenic lakes and rivers, there are various opportunities to take a boat trip and explore the beautiful scenery.

  • Explore the Castles: Cumbria is home to several historic castles that fascinate people of all ages.

Week-Long Itinerary for Cumbria

If you’re planning a week-long trip to Cumbria, here is what your itinerary could look like:

  • Day 1: Explore Carlisle city center and visit Carlisle Castle and Cathedral

  • Day 2: Visit the Lake District National Park and do a lakeside walk or go boating on one of the lakes.

  • Day 3: Visit the Wordsworth Museum, Kendal, and the surrounding area, rich in history.

  • Day 4: Take a scenic drive to Derwentwater and enjoy the views, take a boat trip, hike, or bike the lake shore trail.

  • Day 5: Enjoy a day on a realistic Cumbrian working farm, take a tour in the surrounding areas and have lunch at a local village pub.

  • Day 6: Visit Lake Windermere and explore nearby towns such as Bowness-on-Windermere or Ambleside.

  • Day 7: Head to the west coast to visit St Bees to see the priory and stunning cliffs, visit Egremont, and book a food tour to savor Cumbrian delicacies.

Food and Drink Scene of Cumbria

Cumbria’s food and drink scene feature a variety of local specialties that reflect its farming and fishing cultures. Here are some must-try dishes and beverages:

Must-Try Dishes

  • Cumberland Sausage: A traditional recipe made with pork and spices, typically served with mashed potatoes, beans, and gravy.

  • Grasmere Gingerbread: Sweet and spicy gingerbread named after the village of Grasmere, known for its fragrant gingerbread shop.

  • Kendal Mint Cake: A sugar-rich mint-flavored confectionery that was a favorite of Sir Edmund Hillary during his Everest ascent.

Local Beverages

  • Jennings Brewery: One of the oldest breweries in Cumbria, producing award-winning traditional ales

  • The Lakes Distillery: A modern distillery producing premium gin, vodka, and other spirits, with tours available.

Where to Stay in Cumbria: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Cumbria offers a wide range of accommodations for all budgets and tastes. Here are some of the best places to stay in the area:

  • The Lyth Valley Country Inn: A cozy inn located in a tranquil countryside village, offering high-quality accommodations and fresh local cuisine.

  • The Samling: A luxurious country house hotel located amidst stunning landscapes.

  • YHA Ambleside: A budget-friendly yet high-quality hostel located in the heart of the Lake District National Park.

What to Buy in Cumbria: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

Cumbria has a rich history of craftsmanship, and there are several authentic souvenirs and products you can bring home from the county:

  • Solway Glass: locally produced, high-quality glassware, available in various colors, and varying designs.

  • Coniston Brewing Company: original, handcrafted beers made with local ingredients and water from the famous Coniston water.

  • Maxed-Up Mugs & Tees: Handmade mugs and t-shirts by Cumbrian artist Max Tannahill, featuring designs inspired by the region’s wildlife and landscapes.

Local Culture and Customs of Cumbria

Cumbria is famous for its friendly and welcoming locals, who take pride in their culture and traditions. Some of the region’s cultural customs include:

  • Singing and performing at the Cumbrian Folk Festival

  • Joining in at the Cumberland and Westmorland Wrestling Championships

  • Watching farmer’s competitions such as Sheep Dog Trials or a village fair and flock shows

Best Time to Visit Cumbria

Cumbria is a year-round destination with something to offer in every season. However, the best time to visit depends on your interests and preferred activities.

  • Spring/Summer: Great for outdoor adventures, water sports, and garden tours with pleasant temperatures and long days.

  • Autumn: Best for leaf peeping and hiking in the mountains in cooler temperatures and spectacular colors.

  • Winter: Ideal for winter activities like snowshoeing, skiing, and visiting Christmas markets.

Do’s and Don’ts of Cumbria

Do:

  • Be respectful of local customs and traditions.

  • Take care when hiking and biking in the lake district, as weather conditions can change quickly.

  • Try the local specialties like Cumberland sausages, gingerbread, and Kendal mint cake.

Don’t:

  • Litter or damage the natural beauty of Cumbria.

  • Ignore safety precautions, especially when undertaking outdoor adventures.

  • Be afraid to ask the locals for recommendations on what to do and where to eat.

Safety and Health

Safety in Cumbria

Cumbria has a reputation as a friendly and welcoming place, but visitors should still take steps to remain safe during their stay.

  • Always lock your car and keep valuables out of sight.

  • Stay safe on the roads, especially when driving in hilly, rural areas.

  • Be aware of weather conditions when hiking, biking, or participating in outdoor adventures.

Health in Cumbria

Cumbria has good medical facilities, but visitors should still take precautions to stay healthy:

  • Drink plenty of water to stay hydrated, especially when undertaking outdoor activities.

  • Carry a first aid kit when hiking or biking.

  • Wear sunscreen and cover up in hot weather to avoid sunburn.

Getting to Cumbria: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Cumbria

The nearest airports to Cumbria are Manchester Airport and Liverpool John Lennon Airport, both located around 100 miles away.

Bus Stations Near Cumbria

Various bus services connect Cumbria to the neighboring regions, such as Stagecoach and National Express.

Other Options

There are several train stations all around Cumbria, making it a convenient destination for travelers arriving by train. Alternatively, you can hire a car and explore the region at your own pace.

How to Get Around in Cumbria

Getting around in Cumbria is easy, with several transportation options available:

  • Bike: Hire a bike and explore the lakes and countryside via bike trails.

  • Train: take in the scenic views on your way to different regions of Cumbria.

  • Bus: Regional busses are available at reasonable costs to most attraction sites.

  • Hire a Car: A popular option, Cumbria is well serviced by many hire car companies, providing great flexibility.

Where to Go Next from Cumbria: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

Cumbria is well-connected to neighboring regions and exciting excursions. Nearby destinations within easy reach include:

  • Manchester: A vibrant and cultural city featuring world-class museums, galleries, theaters, and shopping destinations.

  • Liverpool: A city famous for its iconic waterfront, Beatles tours, and cultural landmarks.

  • Yorkshire Dales National Park: A mountainous region famous for its scenic beauty and hiking trails.

Conclusion

Cumbria is an exceptional travel destination offering a unique blend of stunning natural scenery, fascinating history, and refreshing cultural experiences. The county has so much to offer, from outdoor adventures to cultural attractions and food & drink scene. Plan your trip now, and enjoy all that Cumbria has to offer!
