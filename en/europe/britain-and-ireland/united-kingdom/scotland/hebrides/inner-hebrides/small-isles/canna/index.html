Introduction to Canna

Nestled in the Small Isles of Scotland’s Inner Hebrides, Canna is a small but picturesque island that offers a perfect blend of history, culture, and natural beauty. With a population of only 18 residents, this island is an idyllic getaway for anyone looking for a peaceful escape from the bustling city life.

List of Places to Visit in Canna

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

Canna is steeped in history and culture, evident from its numerous museums and historical sites, such as:

  • Canna House: Built in 1905, this well-preserved Edwardian house serves as a museum dedicated to showcasing the life of the island’s past residents and their cultural heritage. Visitors can explore the house’s many rooms, including a library, drawing-room, and dining room, all restored with original decor and furniture.
  • Achaidh Farmhouse: This farmhouse was restored and transformed into a museum, depicting the typical lifestyle of a Hebridean farmer around the 1930s. Visitors can explore the house, and the surrounding croft, learning about the traditional farming methods and the production of wool.
  • St. Edward’s Chapel: This ancient chapel dates back to the 13th century and stands as a testament to the island’s religious heritage. The chapel contains well-preserved carvings and decoration, including a wooden altar, which is believed to be from the Viking era.

Natural Attractions of Canna: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

Canna’s stunning landscapes and stunning views make it a paradise for nature enthusiasts. Here are a few outdoor attractions to explore:

  • Canna Beaches: Canna boasts some of Scotland’s most beautiful beaches, including Sanday Bay Beach, Canna Harbour Beach, and Kilbyrnek Bay Beach. With turquoise blue waters and lusciously green landscapes, these beaches are perfect spots for swimming, sunbathing, and relaxing.
  • Wildlife Spotting: Canna’s rich ecological diversity provides a chance to spot various animals, including otters, puffins, whales, eagles, and seals.
  • Canna Walks: For those seeking adventure, Canna’s many walks and hikes offer breathtaking views of the island’s surroundings. The circular Canna Trail is a 7.5 km route that provides stunning views of the neighboring islands of Small Isles. Additionally, visitors can also explore the Cuillin mountains of nearby Skye Island.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Canna

For those wanting to explore the island on foot or cycle, there are a few recommended routes to take:

  • Canna Trail: As mentioned earlier, the Canna Trail is a 7.5 km loop that takes you across the island’s different terrains. The trail begins and ends at Canna Pier, passing through various landmarks like the harbor, the chapel, and the farmstead.
  • Comraich Loop: This 4.5 km trail takes visitors to the heart of the island across a hilltop offering scenic, panoramic views from every angle.
  • Biking Trails: Cyclists can rent a bicycle from the island’s stores and explore the lush landscapes at their own pace.

Best Things to Do in Canna: Activities and Tours

Apart from the natural and cultural attractions, Canna offers various other options for fun and adventure:

  • Boat Trips: Visitors can go on guided boat tours around the island to spot sea eagles and seals, while enjoying the stunning views of the island from the sea.
  • Kayaking: Canna’s serene waters make it the perfect destination for kayaking enthusiasts who can rent a kayak and paddle their way around the island’s many coves and beaches.
  • Fishing: For those interested in angling, there are various opportunities to fish locally with permits.

Week-Long Itinerary for Canna

If you’re planning to spend a week on the island, here’s an itinerary to help you make the most of your stay:

Food and Drink Scene of Canna

Canna’s local cuisine is heavily influenced by its traditional Scottish roots. Here are a few must-try dishes and beverages that capture the essence of the island:

Must-Try Dishes

  • Cullen Skink: A Scottish soup made from smoked haddock, potatoes, and onions.
  • Haggis: A savory pudding containing sheep’s heart, liver, and lungs, mixed with onions, suet, and spices.
  • Scottish salmon: Freshly caught salmon from the surrounding waters is a popular dish among the locals.

Local Beverages

  • Whiskey: Scotland’s national drink, with a multitude of distilleries scattered in every corner of the country.
  • Real Ale: Produced locally by the Isle of Skye Brewery, featuring various flavors and styles.

Where to Stay in Canna: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Canna offers plenty of affordable and comfortable accommodation options for tourists:

  • Canna Campsite: Provides camping facilities for visitors looking to experience nature in its true element.
  • Tighard Guest House: A traditional guest house that offers cozy rooms with premium amenities and delightful Scottish hospitality.
  • Canna House: Visitors can rent rooms at this historic Edwardian house-turned-museum, offering a unique stay experience.

What to Buy in Canna: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

Canna’s local shops offer various souvenirs and products for tourists to take back home:

  • Canna Crafts Shop: A local store that offers bespoke handicrafts and artwork made from locally sourced materials.
  • Canna Co-op: A village store that offers locally produced goods such as baked goods, seasonal vegetables, and fruit.

Local Culture and Customs of Canna

Canna’s cultural heritage is defined by its ties to the traditional Scottish identity. The locals are proud of their heritage and maintain their traditions by celebrating various events throughout the year. These include:

  • Coll Pottery Festival: A yearly festival that celebrates the island’s art, crafts, and local pottery in Coll, a nearby island.
  • Canna Music Festival: A summer music festival that attracts visitors from across Scotland to the island.

Best Time to Visit Canna

The island is a delight to visit throughout the year, but the best time to go is during the summer months (June-August) when the weather is warm, and the island’s flora and fauna are at their full bloom. However, it’s essential to check the weather reports before planning your trip, as the island’s remoteness and harsh weather can often create inconveniences.

Do’s and Don’ts of Canna

Do:

  • Wear comfortable walking shoes as the island’s topography can be challenging.
  • Respect the island’s fragile ecological balance and maintain the surroundings clean.
  • Take part in the local rituals and traditions when visiting the island.

Don’t:

  • Disturb the local flora and fauna, particularly the seabirds and seals.
  • Build bonfires on the island’s beaches without permission.
  • Litter the surroundings and leave the trash in the designated garbage bins.

Safety and Health

Safety in Canna

Canna’s remoteness makes it a relatively safe destination, with crime and accidents being infrequent. However, tourists should take the following precautions:

  • Be careful when exploring the island’s rocky terrains and wear appropriate footwear.
  • Always carry a charged mobile phone in case of an emergency, and inform the coast guard before going offshore for water activities.

Health in Canna

There is no medical facility on Canna, and medical emergencies are often transferred to the mainland. It’s essential to carry necessary medicines and first aid kits as a precautionary measure.

Getting to Canna: Transportation Options and Logistics

Canna’s remoteness makes reaching the island slightly tricky, but the following options are available:

  • Ferry: A ferry service plies from Mallaig, the nearest town, to Canna. The ferry runs from May to September, and the journey takes two to three hours.
  • Charter boats: Visitors can also rent a private charter boat from the nearby islands to reach Canna.

Airports Near Canna

The nearest airport to Canna is Glasgow International Airport, which is about 240 km away. From there, visitors can take a train or bus to Mallaig and take the ferry to the island.

Bus Stations Near Canna

The nearest bus station to Canna is in Mallaig. However, visitors can also take the bus to Edinburgh’s city center and take the train to Mallaig.

How to Get Around in Canna

Canna is a small island, and visitors can explore most of it on foot. Bicycles can also be rented from the local shops for those wishing to cover more distance.

Where to Go Next from Canna: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

Canna’s proximity to other islands and towns in the Inner Hebrides makes it a starting point for further exploration of the region. Visitors can consider visiting the following destinations:

  • Isle of Skye: Home to the famous Cuillin mountains and the beautiful Fairy Pools, the Isle of Skye is accessible by ferry and offers visitors a chance to explore more of Scotland’s natural beauty.
  • Mallaig: A nearby town that can be reached by ferry and offers several shopping and dining options.
  • Small Isles: A group of four other islands scattered around Canna that offer unique cultural experiences and stunning natural landscapes.

Conclusion

Canna, with its picturesque landscapes and rich cultural history, offers visitors a unique experience of Scotland’s Inner Hebrides. With numerous outdoor adventures and numerous cultural attractions, the island caters to every individual’s interests. Whether it’s basking on the beach, exploring historical sites or going on a hike, a visit to Canna is a must-try for those looking for a serene escape.
