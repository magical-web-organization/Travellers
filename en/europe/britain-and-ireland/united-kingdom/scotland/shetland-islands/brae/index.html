Explore the Best of Brae: A Comprehensive Tour Guide

Brae is located on the northern coast of the Shetland Islands and is known for its stunning natural beauty and rich cultural heritage. From outdoor adventures to museums and historical sites, Brae has something to offer everyone. In this comprehensive tour guide, we’ll explore the best places to visit, the hiking and biking routes, the best things to do, and where to stay and what to eat.

Introduction to Brae

Brae, with its stunning rugged coastline, towering cliffs, and lush countryside, is a paradise for nature lovers. The town itself is a bustling hub of activity, with a range of shops, cafes, and restaurants to explore.

List of Places to Visit in Brae

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • Shetland Museum and Archives: This museum showcases Shetland’s unique heritage, culture, and natural history. It’s a must-visit for anyone interested in learning about the local traditions and customs.

  • Scalloway Castle: Built in 1600, this ruined castle is a fascinating glimpse into Shetland’s past. It’s a popular spot for history buffs and photographers.

  • St. Magnus Cathedral: Widely regarded as one of the finest medieval buildings in Scotland, the St Magnus Cathedral is a UNESCO World Heritage site and a must-visit for architecture enthusiasts.

Natural Attractions of Brae: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • Esha Ness Cliffs: The towering cliffs of Esha Ness offer stunning views of the rugged coastline and the North Sea. It’s a popular spot for bird watching and hiking.

  • Muckle Flugga Lighthouse: Located on the northernmost tip of Unst, the Muckle Flugga Lighthouse is a remarkable feat of engineering and a great spot for photography.

  • Braehead Local Nature Reserve: This nature reserve is home to a diverse range of flora and fauna, including otters, seals, and a variety of bird species. Visitors can explore the area on foot or by bike.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Brae

Brae offers a range of hiking and biking routes, each with its unique charms. Here are some of the best ones:

  • St. Ninian’s Isle: This circular hiking trail is known for its stunning views of the North Sea and the Shetland Islands.

  • Eshaness cliffs walk: The Eshaness cliffs walk is a challenging but rewarding hike that takes you through some of the most breathtaking landscapes in Brae.

  • Braehead local nature reserve trail: The trail through Braehead Local Nature Reserve is an easy and enjoyable walk that’s suitable for all ages and abilities.

Best Things to Do in Brae: Activities and Tours

  • Whale and Dolphin Watching Tour: Take a boat tour to see the majestic whales and dolphins that call the North Sea home.

  • Fishing: The waters around Brae are teeming with fish, making it a popular spot for fishing enthusiasts.

  • Local Crafts Tour: Explore the rich heritage of Shetland’s craft traditions, including wool and knitwear, weaving, and pottery.

Week-Long Itinerary for Brae

Day 1 Explore the Shetland Museum and Archives

Food and Drink Scene of Brae

Must-Try Dishes

  • Shetland Reestit Mutton Soup: A traditional Shetland dish made with lamb, potatoes, and root vegetables.

  • Fish and Chips: Freshly caught fish is battered and fried to perfection, served with a side of chips.

  • Lingonberry Tart: A delicious dessert made with locally sourced lingonberries.

Local Beverages

  • Shetland Ale: A refreshing and flavorful beer made with local ingredients.

  • Shetland Black: A rich and malty beer with a hint of sweetness.

Where to Stay in Brae: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

  • Brae Hotel: This cozy hotel offers comfortable accommodation and a range of amenities, including a bar and restaurant.

  • Brae Guesthouse: This welcoming guesthouse is perfect for budget travelers, offering comfortable rooms and basic amenities.

  • Hillydale Bed and Breakfast: This charming bed and breakfast offers clean and comfortable rooms with stunning views of the surrounding countryside.

What to Buy in Brae: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

  • Shetland Knitwear: Shetland is famous for its wool and knitwear, so be sure to pick up a cozy sweater or hat to bring home.

  • Pottery and Ceramics: Shetland is home to a thriving community of potters and ceramic artists, so look out for unique pieces to bring home.

Local Culture and Customs of Brae

The people of Brae are friendly and welcoming, with a rich cultural heritage that they are proud to share with visitors. Like most places in Scotland, traditional music and dance are an integral part of the local culture.

Best Time to Visit Brae

The best time to visit Brae is during the summer months (June to August) when the weather is mild and sunny, and the days are long.

Do’s and Don’ts of Brae

Do:

  • Respect the local customs and traditions.

  • Try the local cuisine and beverages.

  • Be prepared for changes in weather conditions.

Don’t:

  • Litter or damage the natural landscape.

  • Disturb wildlife or plant life.

  • Disrespect local people or their beliefs.

Safety and Health

Safety in Brae

Brae is a safe place to visit, but visitors should take normal precautions, like locking doors and windows and not leaving valuables unattended.

Health in Brae

Visitors should have comprehensive travel insurance and be up to date on routine vaccinations.

Getting to Brae: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Brae

  • Sumburgh Airport: Located on the southern tip of the Shetland Islands, Sumburgh Airport is the closest airport to Brae.

Bus Stations Near Brae

  • Lerwick Bus Station: located in the town of Lerwick, Lerwick Bus Station is the main hub for bus services throughout the Shetland Islands.

Other Options

  • Ferry: Visitors can take a ferry from Aberdeen to Lerwick, and then transfer to a local bus or hire a car.

How to Get Around in Brae

Visitors can explore Brae on foot, by bike or hire a car.

Where to Go Next from Brae: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

  • Lerwick: The bustling capital of the Shetland Islands, Lerwick offers a range of exciting cultural attractions and outdoor adventures.

  • Unst: The northernmost island in the Shetland archipelago, Unst is known for its rugged natural beauty and rich Viking heritage.

Conclusion

Brae is a hidden gem in the Shetland Islands, offering stunning natural beauty, rich cultural heritage, and a range of exciting outdoor adventures and activities. Whether you’re looking to explore the local museums and historical sites, hike through towering cliffs, or sample the local cuisine and beverages, Brae has something for everyone. Start planning your trip today and discover the magic of this hidden gem in Scotland.
