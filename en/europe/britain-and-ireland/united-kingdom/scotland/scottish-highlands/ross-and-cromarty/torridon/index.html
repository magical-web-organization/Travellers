Introduction to Torridon

Nestled in the heart of Scottish Highlands, Torridon is a paradise for nature lovers and adventure seekers. This remote region boasts some of Scotland’s most stunning landscapes, including towering Munros, sparkling lochs, rugged coastlines, and ancient pine forests. Whether you’re looking to hike, bike, kayak or just escape the hustle and bustle of city life, Torridon has something for everyone. In this guide, we’ll take a close look at what makes Torridon so special, explore the top places to visit, and share insider tips on how to make the most of your trip.

List of Places to Visit in Torridon

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • Torridon Countryside Centre: This visitor center provides information on the region’s natural heritage, including its geology, ecology, and wildlife. The center also houses an exhibition on the area’s human history, from prehistoric times to the present day. Entrance is free.

  • Inverewe Garden & Estate: This unique garden, located on the shores of Loch Ewe, features a collection of exotic plants from around the world, thanks to its unusual microclimate influenced by the Gulf Stream. Visitors can also explore the estate’s woodland trails, views of the loch, and a cafe with locally-made cakes and drinks.

  • Applecross Heritage Centre: Located in the picturesque fishing village of Applecross, this center tells the story of the area’s Gaelic-speaking community and its fishing, crofting and shepherding traditions. The center features a range of interactive exhibits, including a recreated croft house and fishing cottage. Entrance fee is £3.

Natural Attractions of Torridon: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • Beinn Eighe National Nature Reserve: This nature reserve, stretching over 48 square miles, includes ancient Caledonian pine forests, scree-covered mountain slopes, and tranquil lochs. It is home to rare wildlife species such as golden eagles, pine martens, and Scottish crossbill birds. There are several hiking trails available to explore, including the famous Beinn Eighe Ridge Trail.

  • Liathach Mountain: Known as one of Scotland’s most challenging Munros, Liathach is also one of the most rewarding. The mountain offers incredible views of the Torridon Hills and the Lochs below, as well as a chance to test your scrambling and mountaineering skills. Experienced hikers can tackle the full ridge walk, while others can opt for the easier routes.

  • Shieldaig Peninsula Coastal Drive: This scenic drive takes you through the breathtaking coastal villages of Torridon and Shieldaig, providing panoramic views of the sea, mountains, and islands. Keep your camera handy for the photo opportunities!

Hiking/Biking Routes of Torridon

Torridon is known for its challenging but rewarding hiking trails, ranging from easy strolls to steep, rocky mountains. The following are some of the most popular hiking and biking routes in the region:

  • Beinn Eighe Ridge Walk: This 7-mile route, consisting of two peaks, is a must-do for any experienced hiker. The trailhead starts at the visitor center, and it takes approximately 8-10 hours to complete.

  • Torridon Loop Trail: This 12-mile circular route starts and finishes in the village of Torridon and takes you through some of the area’s most scenic points of interest, including Beinn Alligin and the coastline. It can be done in one day or split into two for a more leisurely adventure.

  • Redpoint Mountain Bike Trail - North Coast 500: This 7-mile mountain bike route takes you through the Red Point Beach and its surrounding woodland. You’ll encounter steep climbs, fast descents, technical sections, and plenty of stunning views.

Best Things to Do in Torridon: Activities and Tours

  • Kayaking on Loch Torridon: One of the best ways to experience the stunning scenery of the highlands is by gliding across the water in a kayak. You can rent a kayak from one of the local providers or join a guided tour to explore the hidden bays, islands, and wildlife up close.

  • Wildlife Watching: Torridon is home to a variety of rare and endangered species, including red deer, otters, seals, and sea eagles. You can take a guided wildlife watching tour to increase your chances of spotting these creatures in their natural habitats.

  • Whisky Tasting: Scotland is famous for its whisky, and Torridon has its own distillery, called Torridon Shieling, producing a range of single malt whiskies. Take a tour of the distillery, learn about the whisky-making process, and sample some of the best whiskies of the region.

Week-Long Itinerary for Torridon

Day 1: Arrival in Inverness and drive to Torridon; explore the Torridon Hills and Beinn Eighe National Nature Reserve

Day 2: Hiking along the Beinn Eighe Ridge Walk; viewpoint of Loch Maree from the summit

Day 3: Cycling the Torridon Loop Trail; visit the Inverewe Garden & Estate

Day 4: Sea kayaking on Loch Torridon; beach picnic at Red Point Beach

Day 5: Visit the Applecross Heritage Center and enjoy lunch or dinner at the famous Applecross Inn

Day 6: Hiking along Liathach Mountain; wildlife watching tour

Day 7: Whisky tasting at Torridon Shieling Distillery; Departure.

Food and Drink Scene of Torridon

Must-Try Dishes

  • Haggis: A traditional Scottish dish made of sheep’s heart, liver, and lungs mixed with oatmeal and spices.

  • Cullen Skink: A creamy soup made of smoked haddock, potatoes, and onions.

  • Cranachan: A dessert made of whipped cream, raspberries, and toasted oatmeal.

Local Beverages

  • Single Malt Whisky: Scotland’s most famous drink, made from malted barley and water. Torridon Shieling Distillery produces a range of single malt whiskies, including the award-winning Ardlair.

  • Real Ale: There are several breweries in the area producing craft beers, including the Lochcarron Brewery and the Isle of Skye Brewery. Try the Redpoint Ale, named after the nearby beach.

Where to Stay in Torridon: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

  • The Torridon: This luxury hotel sits on the shores of Loch Torridon and offers a range of accommodation options, from elegant rooms to self-catering apartments, all with stunning views of the surrounding scenery. The hotel also features a 3 AA Rosette restaurant showcasing local produce.

  • Torridon Youth Hostel: If you’re on a budget, this hostel provides affordable accommodation in dormitory-style or private rooms. It also has a well-equipped kitchen and a lounge area with a fireplace.

  • Kinlochewe Hotel and Bunkhouse: This family-run hotel and bunkhouse is located in the heart of Kinlochewe village and offers comfortable en-suite rooms and affordable bunk beds for backpackers. The hotel menu features dishes made with locally sourced ingredients.

What to Buy in Torridon: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

  • Knitwear: Scotland is known for its traditional woolen knitwear, including jumpers, hats, gloves, and scarves. The Torridon area has several locally owned stores where you can find high-quality knitted items.

  • Whisky: Torridon Shieling Distillery produces a range of single malt whiskies, including aged versions, that make perfect gifts for whisky lovers.

Local Culture and Customs of Torridon

The people of Torridon are proud of their rich cultural heritage and will often greet visitors with a warm welcome. Some of the cultural customs include:

  • Gaelic Language: Gaelic is still spoken in the Torridon area, and some of the signs and place names reflect the language’s heritage.

  • Traditional Music and Dance: The region has a strong tradition of folk music and dance, and there are several festivals and events featuring local musicians throughout the year.

Best Time to Visit Torridon

The best time to visit Torridon is during the summer months, from May to September, when the weather is milder, and the days are longer. However, this is also peak tourist season, so be prepared for crowds and book accommodation early. The shoulder seasons of spring and autumn can be just as beautiful and less crowded.

Do’s and Don’ts of Torridon

Do:

  • Do pack appropriate clothing for the Scottish weather, including waterproofs and warm layers.

  • Do respect the area’s natural environment by not leaving litter or damaging plants or wildlife.

  • Do drive on the left side of the road.

Don’t:

  • Don’t ignore warning signs or advice from locals regarding the terrain and weather conditions.

  • Don’t disturb wildlife or their habitats.

  • Don’t take rocks or plants as souvenirs.

Safety and Health

Safety in Torridon

  • Be aware of the risks associated with outdoor activities, including hiking and kayaking, and take necessary precautions, such as checking weather reports, wearing proper footwear and clothing, and carrying maps and safety equipment.

  • Beware of sudden changes in weather conditions, especially in the mountains.

  • Avoid walking alone in remote areas, especially at night.

Health in Torridon

  • The tap water in Torridon is safe to drink.

  • The area has several medical practices and pharmacies, but emergency services may take longer to arrive in remote areas.

  • Watch out for ticks when walking in wooded areas and check yourself for bites.

Getting to Torridon: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Torridon

The nearest airports to Torridon are:

  • Inverness Airport (68 miles)

  • Glasgow International Airport (139 miles)

Bus Stations Near Torridon

There is a limited bus service to and from Torridon. The nearest bus stations are:

  • Gairloch (20 miles)

  • Kinlochewe (7 miles)

Other Options

The most convenient way to explore the Scottish Highlands is by car. You can rent a car in Inverness or Glasgow and drive to Torridon. The journey takes approximately two hours from Inverness and three hours from Glasgow.

How to Get Around in Torridon

The best way to explore Torridon is on foot, by bike, or by kayak. There is a limited public bus service which connects the villages, but it’s infrequent. Taxis and car rentals are available but can be expensive.

Where to Go Next from Torridon: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

  • Skye Island: Known for its rugged landscapes, stunning coastline, and ancient history, Skye Island is a popular day-trip destination from Torridon.

  • Wester Ross Coastal Trail: This scenic driving route takes you along the beautiful coast of Wester Ross, passing through charming villages, sandy beaches, and historic ruins.

  • Loch Ness: The most famous loch in Scotland, known for its monster sightings and fascinating history, is only a two-hour drive from Torridon.

Conclusion

Torridon is a hidden gem in the Scottish Highlands, a place where nature, culture, and adventure merge into a unique, unforgettable experience. By following this guide, you’ll learn about the best places to visit, the most exciting activities to try, and the local customs to respect. Prepare to be enchanted by this stunning region, and to create memories that will last a lifetime.
