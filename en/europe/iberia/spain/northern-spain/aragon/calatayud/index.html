Calatayud is a charming town located in the heart of Aragon, Northern Spain. It is known for its rich history, stunning natural landscapes, and delicious local cuisine. Whether you’re a history buff, an adventure seeker, or just looking to relax and unwind, Calatayud has something for everyone. In this comprehensive travel guide to Calatayud, we’ll share with you the best places to visit, top attractions, activities, where to stay, what to eat, and more!

Introduction to Calatayud

Calatayud dates back to Roman times, and its history is evident in its well-preserved Roman ruins, including the impressive Roman theater. The town also played an important role during the Moorish occupation of Spain, and its Islamic influence can be seen in the stunning Aljafería Palace and the ancient town walls.

Today, Calatayud is a thriving town with a rich cultural heritage and a thriving tourism industry. Visitors from all over the world come to explore its ancient history, discover its natural beauty, and sample its delicious local cuisine.

List of Places to Visit in Calatayud

Whether you’re interested in history, nature, or gastronomy, Calatayud has plenty of attractions to keep you occupied. Here are some of the top places to visit in Calatayud:

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • Aljafería Palace: This stunning palace dates back to the 11th century and is considered one of the finest examples of Islamic architecture in Spain.
  • Roman Theater: This well-preserved theater, built in the 1st century AD, was once a vibrant hub of activity in ancient Calatayud.
  • Colegiata de Santa Maria: This beautiful 16th-century church is one of the most important religious sites in Calatayud.
  • Calatayud Castle: This imposing fortress dates back to the 9th century and offers stunning views over the town.

Natural Attractions of Calatayud: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • Monasterio de Piedra: This stunning nature reserve is located just outside Calatayud and is famous for its waterfall-filled ravine and lush greenery.
  • Sierra de la Virgen: This mountain range offers spectacular views of Calatayud and the surrounding countryside.
  • The Jiloca River: This beautiful river offers opportunities for kayaking, fishing, and other water-based activities.
  • Parque de la Ruta del Agua: This beautiful park is home to a variety of native plant species and a popular spot for hiking and birdwatching.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Calatayud

Calatayud is a paradise for outdoor enthusiasts, and there are plenty of hiking and biking trails to explore. Here are some of the best routes:

  • Ruta del Agua: This hiking trail takes you through the beautiful Parque de la Ruta del Agua, offering stunning views of the Jiloca River and the surrounding landscape.
  • Vía Verde: This former railway track has been converted into a cycling and hiking trail that takes you through some of the most scenic areas around Calatayud.
  • Sierra de la Virgen: This mountain range offers some of the most challenging hiking and biking trails, with stunning views of the town and the countryside.

Best Things to Do in Calatayud: Activities and Tours

Apart from exploring the historical and natural attractions, there are plenty of things to do in Calatayud. Here are some of the must-do activities:

  • Wine Tasting: Calatayud is known for its excellent wines, and a wine tasting tour is a great way to sample some of the best vintages the town has to offer.
  • Food Tours: Take a gastronomic tour of Calatayud and discover the local cuisine, from traditional tapas to hearty stews.
  • Hot Air Balloon Rides: Soak up the stunning views of Calatayud and its surroundings from high up in the sky with a hot air balloon ride.
  • Festivals: Calatayud hosts a number of festivals throughout the year, including the Fiestas de San Roque and the Semana Santa, which are great ways to experience the town’s culture and traditions.

Week-Long Itinerary for Calatayud

If you’re planning to visit Calatayud for a week, here’s a sample itinerary to help you make the most of your trip:

Day 1: Explore the town’s rich history and visit the Aljaferia Palace and the Roman Theater. Day 2: Head to the Monasterio de Piedra and spend the day exploring the waterfalls and natural beauty of the area. Day 3: Take a hot air balloon ride or go kayaking on the Jiloca River. Day 4: Visit the Colegiata de Santa Maria and the Calatayud Castle. Day 5: Take a wine tasting tour and experience the local flavors. Day 6: Go on a hiking or biking tour and explore the Sierra de la Virgen. Day 7: Attend a local festival or relax and unwind in one of the town’s many coffee shops or bars.

Food and Drink Scene of Calatayud

Calatayud is known for its delicious local cuisine, which draws inspiration from both its Islamic and Christian past. Here are some of the must-try dishes, along with the best places to sample them:

Must-Try Dishes

  • Migas: A traditional Spanish dish made with breadcrumbs, garlic, and olive oil, often served with meat or vegetables. Sample it at El Market.
  • Calatayud Stew: A hearty stew made with chickpeas, potatoes, and meat, often served with rice. Sample it at La Jabonera.
  • Tostadas de ajo: Toast with garlic, oil and ham. Try it at La Taurina.

Local Beverages

Calatayud is also known for its excellent wines, which are produced using a variety of grapes, including Garnacha and Tempranillo. You can sample them at local bodegas (wineries) such as Bodegas San Alejandro and Bodegas Ateca.

Where to Stay in Calatayud: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Calatayud has plenty of accommodation options to suit every budget and taste. Here are some of the best places to stay:

  • Hotel Fornos: A charming hotel located in the heart of Calatayud, close to all the main attractions.
  • Casa Rural La Casa del Tío Americano: A cozy guesthouse situated in the countryside, offering stunning views of the Sierra de la Virgen.
  • Hostal Las Rumbas: A budget-friendly hostel located in the town center, perfect for travelers on a tight budget.

What to Buy in Calatayud: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

If you’re looking to bring back a piece of Calatayud with you, here are some of the best souvenirs and locally made products:

  • Wine: Calatayud’s wines are some of the best in the region, and make the perfect gift or souvenir.
  • Handmade ceramics: Calatayud is known for its traditional pottery, often featuring intricate designs and bright colors.
  • Olive Oil: Spain is famous for its high-quality olive oil, and Calatayud produces some excellent varieties.

Local Culture and Customs of Calatayud

Calatayud has a rich cultural heritage, and locals take pride in their traditions and customs. Here are some things to keep in mind when visiting Calatayud:

  • Respect the siesta period: Many shops and businesses close during the afternoon for a siesta, so plan your activities accordingly.
  • Dress modestly when visiting religious sites: If you plan to visit a church or religious site, dress conservatively out of respect for local customs.
  • Learn some basic Spanish: While many people in Calatayud speak English, it’s always appreciated when visitors make an effort to speak the local language.

Best Time to Visit Calatayud

The best time to visit Calatayud is during the spring and fall, when the weather is mild and comfortable and the town is less crowded than in the peak summer months.

Do’s and Don’ts of Calatayud

Here are some do’s and don’ts to keep in mind when visiting Calatayud:

Do:

  • Try the local cuisine: Calatayud is known for its delicious food and wine, so make sure to sample as much as you can.
  • Explore the natural beauty: Calatayud’s stunning landscapes are not to be missed, so make time for outdoor activities.
  • Learn about the local history: The town’s rich history is evident everywhere you look, so take time to learn about the people who shaped Calatayud.

Don’t:

  • Disrespect religious customs: Calatayud has a strong Catholic heritage, so be respectful when visiting churches and other religious sites.
  • Ignore siesta time: Many shops close during the afternoon for a siesta, so plan your activities accordingly.
  • Park illegally: Parking regulations are strictly enforced in Calatayud, so make sure to abide by the rules to avoid a fine.

Safety and Health

Calatayud is a safe and healthy destination, with excellent medical facilities and a low crime rate. Here are some tips to stay safe and healthy during your trip:

Safety in Calatayud

  • Be aware of pickpockets in crowded areas, such as train stations and busy tourist attractions.
  • Follow the local traffic laws, as driving in Spain can be challenging for foreign visitors.

Health in Calatayud

  • Make sure you have adequate travel insurance, including coverage for medical emergencies.
  • Be aware of the high temperatures in summer and take appropriate measures to prevent heat stroke and dehydration.

Getting to Calatayud: Transportation Options and Logistics

Calatayud is well-connected to the rest of Spain, with easy access to major cities like Madrid and Barcelona. Here are some of the best transportation options:

Airports Near Calatayud

The nearest airports are Zaragoza and Madrid.

Bus Stations Near Calatayud

There are several bus stations in Calatayud, including Estación de Autobuses, offering regular services to major cities in Spain.

Other Options

There are also train services to Calatayud, which connect to Zaragoza, Madrid and Barcelona. Car rentals and taxis are also available.

How to Get Around in Calatayud

Calatayud is a small town, and most of its attractions are within walking distance. However, if you want to explore the surrounding countryside or travel further afield, renting a car or taking a bus or train is the best option.

Where to Go Next from Calatayud: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

Calatayud is a great base from which to explore the surrounding region of Aragon, home to some of Spain’s most beautiful natural landscapes and cultural heritage. Here are some nearby destinations to consider:

  • Zaragoza: The capital of Aragon and home to some of Spain’s most impressive Islamic architecture, including the famous Basilica del Pilar.
  • Teruel: A beautiful town located in the mountains of Aragon, known for its stunning Mudejar architecture and rich history.
  • Soria: An ancient city nestled in the foothills of the Sierra de Cebollera, known for its beautiful gardens and historic monuments.

Conclusion

Calatayud is a beautiful and vibrant town with a rich cultural heritage and plenty of attractions to explore. Whether you’re interested in history, nature, or gastronomy, you’re sure to find plenty to keep you occupied. We hope this comprehensive travel guide to Calatayud has inspired you to visit this incredible destination and discover all it has to offer!
