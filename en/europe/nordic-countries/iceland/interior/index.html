Iceland’s Interior, often referred to as the ‘Real Iceland,’ is the perfect destination for adventurous travelers seeking unique experiences. The region is known for its otherworldly landscapes, active volcanoes, and vast wilderness. But, with so much to explore, planning a trip to Iceland’s Interior can be overwhelming. So, we’ve put together a comprehensive travel guide to help you make the most of your visit.

Introduction to Interior

Iceland’s Interior is a vast, uninhabited wilderness that covers over 40,000 square kilometers. The region is characterized by a rugged landscape of glaciers, mountains, and hot springs, making it a popular destination for outdoor enthusiasts. However, it remains largely untouched by human development, offering a unique opportunity to disconnect from the modern world and immerse yourself in nature.

List of Places to Visit in Interior

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

Despite being largely uninhabited, Iceland’s Interior is home to several cultural and historical attractions worth exploring. The region has a rich folklore steeped in myths and legends, and several museums celebrate the country’s heritage.

  • Þjóðveldisbærinn Stöng: This reconstructed Viking-age farmhouse offers a glimpse into Iceland’s early history.
  • Surtshellir Cave: Located in West Iceland, this lava tube cave is rumored to be the home of trolls and outlaws in Icelandic folklore.
  • The Settlement Center: Located in Borgarnes, this museum showcases the history of Iceland’s settlement and Viking Age.

Natural Attractions of Interior: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

Iceland’s Interior is a playground for outdoor adventurers, with plenty of opportunities to explore volcanoes, glaciers, and hot springs. Whether you enjoy hiking, biking, or driving, the region offers something for everyone.

  • Landmannalaugar: This geothermal wonderland boasts an impressive landscape of hot springs, volcanic craters, and hiking trails.
  • Vatnajökull Glacier: The largest glacier in Europe is home to breathtaking ice formations and stunning ice caves.
  • Hveravellir: This geothermal area is located in the heart of Iceland’s Interior and offers visitors the chance to bathe in hot springs while enjoying the stunning views.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Interior

Iceland’s Interior offers some of the best hiking and biking trails in the world, with options ranging from easy day trips to multi-day excursions.

  • Laugavegur Trail: One of Iceland’s most famous hiking trails, the Laugavegur Trail takes you through some of the country’s most breathtaking scenery.
  • Kerlingarfjöll: This mountain range is a popular hiking destination, with trails suitable for all levels of hikers.
  • Fimmvörðuháls: This challenging hike takes you through glaciers and volcanic landscapes, offering stunning views of waterfalls, mountains, and glaciers along the way.

Best Things to Do in Interior: Activities and Tours

From snowmobiling to glacier hiking to exploring geothermal pools, Iceland’s Interior is full of exciting activities and tours.

  • Jökulsárlón Glacier Lagoon Boat Tour: This tour takes you through the stunning Jökulsárlón Glacier Lagoon, where you can admire the beautiful ice formations and spot seals.
  • Snowmobiling on Vatnajökull Glacier: Experience the thrill of driving a snowmobile on the largest glacier in Europe.
  • Super Jeep Tours: Explore Iceland’s backcountry in a specially designed super jeep, which allows you to reach remote areas inaccessible by regular vehicles.

Food and Drink Scene of Interior

Icelandic cuisine is known for its fresh, locally sourced ingredients and unique flavor combinations. The Interior region offers a range of dining options, from traditional Icelandic cuisine to international fare.

Must-Try Dishes

  • Hangikjöt: Smoked lamb served with boiled potatoes, white sauce, and peas.
  • Skyr: A thick Icelandic yogurt, often served with fresh berries and honey.
  • Rúgbrauð: This traditional Icelandic bread is made with rye flour and baked in the ground using geothermal heat.

Local Beverages

  • Brennivín: This traditional Icelandic schnapps is made from fermented potatoes and flavored with caraway seeds.
  • Icelandic Beer: Try local brews such as Einstök or Borg Brugghús.

Where to Stay in Interior: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

There are a range of accommodation options available in Iceland’s Interior, from luxury hotels to budget-friendly hostels and guesthouses.

  • Hrauneyjar Highland Center: This hotel is located in the heart of Iceland’s Interior and offers comfortable rooms and a restaurant.
  • Hut-To-Hut Hiking: For a unique experience, consider sleeping in one of Iceland’s mountain huts while exploring the Laugavegur Trail.
  • Campgrounds: If you’re on a budget, camping is a popular option in Iceland’s Interior. There are several campsites throughout the region, offering basic facilities and stunning views.

What to Buy in Interior: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

Iceland is known for its unique souvenirs and local products, including handmade woolens, artisanal ceramics, and natural skincare products. Here are some popular items to look out for in Iceland’s Interior:

  • Lopapeysa: This traditional Icelandic woolen sweater is perfect for keeping warm on hikes or as a cozy souvenir.
  • Icelandic Skincare Products: Try locally made skincare products, such as volcanic clay masks or birch-infused toners.
  • Handmade Ceramic Mugs: Icelandic artisans are known for their beautiful ceramics, often inspired by the country’s natural landscapes.

Local Culture and Customs of Interior

Icelandic culture is deeply rooted in Norse mythology and folklore, with a rich history of storytelling and music. The country also has a strong tradition of sustainability and environmentalism, with a focus on preserving its natural resources for future generations.

Best Time to Visit Interior

The best time to visit Iceland’s Interior depends on what you want to do and see. The region is accessible year-round, but weather conditions and daylight hours can vary drastically.

  • Summer (June-August): The summer months offer long daylight hours, mild temperatures, and access to hiking trails and hot springs. However, this is also peak tourist season.

  • Winter (December-February): The winter months offer fewer tourists, a chance to see the Northern Lights, and activities such as snowmobiling and ice caving. However, the weather can be unpredictable and road conditions may be challenging.

Do’s and Don’ts of Interior

Do:

  • Dress in layers and come prepared for changing weather conditions.
  • Respect the natural environment and follow the ‘Leave No Trace’ principles.
  • Take the time to learn about Icelandic culture and customs.

Don’t:

  • Ignore warning signs or advice from locals regarding safety.
  • Disturb wildlife or damage natural habitats.
  • Speak loudly or engage in disruptive behavior in public places.

Safety and Health

Safety in Interior

Iceland’s Interior can be unpredictable and has several hazards you should be aware of, including sudden weather changes, glacial crevasses, and unstable volcanic terrain. Always follow safety guidelines and advice from experienced guides and locals.

Health in Interior

The water in Iceland is safe to drink from taps, streams, and rivers in Iceland’s Interior. However, if you’re hiking or engaging in outdoor activities, it’s essential to pack enough food and water for the duration of your trip.

Getting to Interior: Transportation Options and Logistics

Reykjavik is the gateway to Iceland’s Interior, and there are several transportation options available to reach the region.

Airports Near Interior

  • Reykjavik Airport: The closest airport to the Interior, located in Reykjavik.

Bus Stations Near Interior

  • Reykjavik Bus Terminal: The central bus station for Reykjavik, offering connections to several locations throughout Iceland.

Other Options

  • Car Rental: Renting a car is a popular option for exploring Iceland’s Interior, but be aware that certain roads may only be accessible by 4x4 vehicles.
  • Super Jeep Tours: Many tour operators offer super jeep tours, which allow you to explore the region’s backcountry in a specially designed vehicle.

How to Get Around in Interior

The best way to explore Iceland’s Interior is by renting a car or joining a guided tour. Many areas are only accessible by 4x4 vehicles or require knowledgeable local drivers. Check road conditions and weather forecasts before heading out.

Where to Go Next from Interior: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

If you have additional time after exploring Iceland’s Interior, consider adding some nearby destinations to your itinerary.

  • Westfjords: A remote region in northwestern Iceland, known for its stunning fjords and rugged coastline.
  • Snaefellsnes Peninsula: Located on Iceland’s west coast, this peninsula is known for its dramatic landscapes and abundant wildlife.
  • Reykjavik: Iceland’s capital city, known for its cultural landmarks and vibrant nightlife scene.

Conclusion

Iceland’s Interior is a once-in-a-lifetime destination that offers a unique opportunity to immerse yourself in nature and disconnect from the modern world. Whether you’re exploring geysers, hiking glaciers, or relaxing in hot springs, the region is sure to leave a lasting impression. Use the information in this guide to plan your trip and make the most of your visit to one of the most beautiful places on earth.
