Introduction to Hardanger

Located in the southwestern part of Norway, Hardanger is a breathtakingly beautiful region that attracts thousands of visitors every year. Famous for its fruit orchards, scenic fjords, and crystal-clear water bodies, Hardanger is a popular destination for nature lovers, hikers, bikers, and anyone seeking adventure or relaxation. This travel guide provides a comprehensive overview of Hardanger, including the best places to visit, must-try dishes, where to stay, and how to get around.

List of Places to Visit in Hardanger

Whether you’re a first-time visitor or a seasoned traveler, Hardanger offers a wide range of attractions and landmarks to explore. Here are some of the most popular places to see in Hardanger:

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

  • Hardanger Folk Museum: This open-air museum located in Utne showcases the traditional buildings and way of life of people in the region from the 16th century to the present day. Visitors can see old farmhouses, stables, barns, and boats, and learn about Hardanger’s rich cultural heritage.
  • Agatunet: This medieval cluster of buildings situated in the village of Aga is a well-preserved example of Hardanger’s rural architecture. Visitors can explore the 14 historic houses, museum, and art gallery here.
  • Steinsdalsfossen Waterfall: This majestic waterfall, about 46 meters tall, located in Norheimsund, is one of Hardanger’s most impressive natural landmarks. The unique feature of this waterfall is that you can walk behind the falls and experience the wild thundering of water.

Natural Attractions of Hardanger: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

  • Folgefonna Glacier National Park: This national park is home to the third-largest glacier in Norway and offers breathtaking views of snow-capped mountains, fjords, and valleys. Visitors can enjoy activities such as hiking, skiing, and snowboarding.
  • Hardangervidda National Park: This national park is the largest wilderness area in Northern Europe and is popular with hikers, skiers, and nature lovers. The park is home to wild reindeer, arctic foxes, and other animals, and offers stunning views across the plateau.
  • Hardangerfjord: This fjord is the second-longest in Norway, stretching about 179 kilometers, and is dotted with charming villages, spectacular waterfalls, and imposing mountains. Visitors can enjoy a range of activities, such as kayaking, fishing, and boat tours.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Hardanger

Hardanger is a hiker’s paradise, with numerous trails and routes that offer stunning views of the region’s mountains, fjords, and waterfalls. Below are some of the popular hiking and biking routes in Hardanger:

  • Hardangervidda Plateau: This vast plateau is crisscrossed with several trails that offer panoramic views of the surrounding area. Hikers can explore the plateaus’ barren landscapes, glaciers, and waterfalls.
  • Måbødalen Valley: This seven-kilometer long valley located near Vøringsfossen waterfall is a popular hiking route and offers stunning views of the surrounding mountains and forest.
  • Rallarvegen: This old construction road runs 122 kilometers from Haugastøl to Flåm and is a popular biking route. The trail passes through stunning scenery, such as mountain plateaus, lakes, and waterfalls, and traverses old railway bridges and tunnels.

Best Things to Do in Hardanger: Activities and Tours

  • Fruit Picking: Hardanger is famous for its fruit orchards, and visitors can pick their fruits when in season. Cherries, plums, pears, and apples are among the fruits grown in the region.
  • Fjord Cruise: A fjord cruise is a great way to explore the majestic Hardangerfjord and its surrounding areas. Visitors can enjoy the scenery, take photos, and learn about the area’s history and culture.
  • Skiing and Snowboarding: Hardanger offers several ski resorts, which provide facilities for skiing and snowboarding. Røldal Skisenter in Odda and Voss Resort in Voss are some of the popular ski resorts in Hardanger.

Week-Long Itinerary for Hardanger

Day 1: Explore Bergen and take the Flåm railway to Voss. Day 2: Hike up to Trolltunga in Odda. Day 3: Visit the Hardangerfjord, and take a fjord cruise. Day 4: Visit the Hardangervidda National Park or Folgefonna Glacier National Park for hiking. Day 5: Visit the Hardanger Folk Museum, Agatunet, or Steinsdalsfossen Waterfall. Day 6: Enjoy fruit picking at one of the many orchards in the region. Day 7: Relax in one of the many spas in the region.

Food and Drink Scene of Hardanger

Hardanger’s cuisine is influenced by the region’s natural resources, and visitors can expect to see dishes that celebrate local fruits and fish. Here are some must-try dishes and local beverages:

Must-Try Dishes

  • Smalahove: This traditional Norwegian delicacy is a boiled sheep head, which is considered a delicacy by many locals.
  • Fruit Soup: A sweet soup made from local berries and fruits, such as raspberry, currant, or strawberry. It is served as a dessert.
  • Fårikål: A hearty dish of lamb and cabbage that is traditionally served in the fall.

Local Beverages

  • Cider: Hardanger is known for its delicious apple cider, made from local apples and pears.
  • Aquavit: A traditional Norwegian spirit, often flavored with herbs and spices.

Where to Stay in Hardanger: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Hardanger offers a range of accommodation options, from budget-friendly hostels to luxury hotels and guesthouses. Here are some popular options:

  • Hotel Ullensvang: This historic hotel in Lofthus dates back to 1846 and offers stunning views of the Hardangerfjord, as well as an indoor and outdoor pool, spa, and restaurant.
  • Hardanger Hostel: This budget-friendly hostel in Odda provides clean and comfortable accommodation, including private rooms and dorms.
  • Fugl Fønix Hotel: This cozy hotel in Ullensvang offers modern rooms with breathtaking views of the surrounding mountains and orchards.

What to Buy in Hardanger: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

Hardanger is known for its high-quality handicrafts, such as jewelry, pottery, and textiles. Visitors can also buy local produce, such as apples, pears, and cider. Here are some popular souvenirs and products to bring home:

  • Hardanger Embroidery: A traditional form of Norwegian embroidery known for its intricate floral patterns and bright colors.
  • Wooden Crafts: Hardanger is famous for its wooden handicrafts, such as bowls, spoons, and carvings.
  • Cider: Local cider is a popular souvenir and makes a great gift for friends and family back home.

Local Culture and Customs of Hardanger

Hardanger has a rich cultural history, encompassing farming, fishing, and craftsmanship. Visitors can expect to see traditions, such as folk music and dance, as well as colorful Viking-inspired costumes. The region is also known for its friendly locals, who are proud of their heritage and happy to share it with visitors.

Best Time to Visit Hardanger

The best time to visit Hardanger depends on your interests and preferred activities. Spring and summer (May-September) are the best times for outdoor activities, such as hiking, biking, and fruit picking. Autumn (October-November) is an excellent time to visit to see the fall colors and enjoy the seasonal dishes. Winter (December-March) is the best time for skiing and snowboarding enthusiasts.

Do’s and Don’ts of Hardanger

Do:

  • Respect locals and their customs.
  • Dress in layers, as weather conditions can change quickly.
  • Explore the region’s natural and cultural heritage.
  • Hike and bike responsibly.

Don’t:

  • Litter or damage the natural surroundings.
  • Ignore warning signs or safety instructions.
  • Wear inappropriate clothing or shoes for outdoor activities.
  • Show disrespect toward locals or their traditions.

Safety and Health

Safety in Hardanger

Hardanger is a safe place to visit, with low crime rates. However, visitors should always take care when hiking or biking and follow safety guidelines. In case of an emergency, dial 112 for assistance.

Health in Hardanger

Norway has a high standard of healthcare, and visitors can expect to receive excellent medical care if needed. Visitors should be up-to-date with routine vaccinations and check with their doctor about additional vaccines or medications.

Getting to Hardanger: Transportation Options and Logistics

Airports Near Hardanger

Bergen Airport Flesland is the closest airport to Hardanger, located about 98 kilometers away. From there, visitors can take a bus, train, or rental car to reach their destination.

Bus Stations Near Hardanger

The main bus station in Hardanger is located in Odda, which offers routes to other cities and towns in the region, including Voss and Bergen.

Other Options

Car rental is another convenient option for visitors who want to explore Hardanger at their own pace.

How to Get Around in Hardanger

Renting a car is the most convenient way to get around Hardanger, but visitors can also use buses, taxis, or bicycles. Many hotels and guesthouses also offer shuttle services.

Where to Go Next from Hardanger: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

  • Bergen: Norway’s second-largest city and gateway to the fjords, located about 115 km away from Hardanger.
  • Flåm: A charming village located deep in the Aurlandsfjord, about 100 km away from Hardanger. It is famous for the Flåm Railway, which offers stunning views of the surrounding mountains and waterfalls.
  • Geirangerfjord: One of the most stunning fjords in Norway and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. It is located about 310 km away from Hardanger.

Conclusion

Hardanger is a picturesque region with a rich cultural heritage and stunning natural scenery. Whether you’re looking for outdoor adventures, cultural landmarks, or gastronomic experiences, Hardanger won’t disappoint. We hope this travel guide helps you plan your upcoming trip and provides all the information you need to make the most of your visit.
