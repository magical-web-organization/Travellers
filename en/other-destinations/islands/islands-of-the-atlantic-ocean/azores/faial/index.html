Introduction to Faial

Faial is a beautiful island situated in the Azores, an archipelago of nine volcanic islands in the middle of the Atlantic Ocean. Commonly referred to as the “Blue Island,” Faial is known for its stunning natural beauty, rich history, and vibrant culture. With its charming fishing villages, lush forests, volcanic craters, and whitewashed houses adorned with blue stripes, Faial is a hidden gem waiting to be discovered. This guide will introduce you to the best Faial has to offer and help you plan your dream trip to this special place.

List of Places to Visit in Faial

Faial is full of attractions that cater to a wide range of tastes and interests, including culturally important museums and historical places, natural attractions, hiking/biking routes, and much more. Let’s take a closer look at each category.

Culturally Important Attractions: Museums and Historical Places

Faial has a long and fascinating history that is reflected in its many museums and historical places. Here are some of the must-visit places for history buffs and culture lovers:

  • Horta Regional Museum: This museum offers a glimpse into the island’s past, with exhibits on whaling, the art of scrimshaw, and the Azores’ role in World War II.
  • Peter’s Cafe Sport: A legendary meeting place for sailors and adventurers, Peter’s Cafe Sport has been a Faial institution since 1918.
  • Capelinhos Volcano Interpretation Centre: This modern museum tells the story of the 1957 volcanic eruption that transformed Faial’s landscape and offers a unique perspective on geology and the power of nature.

Natural Attractions of Faial: Outdoor Adventures and Scenic Views

Faial’s natural beauty is unmatched in its variety. From picturesque beaches to verdant forests to volcanic craters, there is plenty to explore and discover. Here are some of the top natural attractions of Faial:

  • Caldeira: This massive volcanic crater is the defining feature of Faial’s landscape. You can hike around the rim and enjoy stunning views of the island.
  • Monte da Guia: This small peninsula is home to a lighthouse and a beautiful natural pool that is ideal for swimming and sunbathing.
  • Ponta dos Capelinhos: This stunningly beautiful cape was created by the 1957 eruption and offers breathtaking views of the Atlantic and the island’s rugged coastline.

Hiking/Biking Routes of Faial

Faial is a paradise for hikers and bikers, with a vast network of trails that take you through forests, along the coast, and up to the island’s highest peaks. Here are some of the most popular routes:

  • Caldeira Loop Trail: This 12km circular route takes you around the rim of the Caldeira crater, offering breathtaking views and a glimpse into the island’s geological history.
  • Faial da Terra Trail: This 12km route takes you through a picturesque valley dotted with waterfalls and lush vegetation, culminating in a stunning ocean view.
  • Capelinhos Trail: This 5km route takes you through the volcanic landscape created by the 1957 eruption, offering a unique perspective on nature’s power and resilience.

Best Things to Do in Faial: Activities and Tours

Faial offers a wide range of activities and tours for visitors of all ages and interests. Whether you want to go whale watching, explore the island’s culinary scene, or learn the art of traditional Azorean handicrafts, there is something for everyone. Here are some of the best things to do in Faial:

  • Whale watching: Faial is one of the best places in the world to see whales in their natural habitat. Several tour operators offer boat trips that take you close to these magnificent creatures.
  • Scuba diving: Faial’s crystal-clear waters are perfect for scuba diving. There are several dive centers on the island that offer guided tours and equipment rental.
  • Azorean culinary tour: Faial is known for its delicious seafood and traditional Azorean dishes. Take a culinary tour and sample the island’s best food and wine.
  • Handicraft workshop: Learn the art of Azorean handicrafts, such as weaving, embroidery, and pottery, from local artisans.

Week-Long Itinerary for Faial

If you’re planning a week-long trip to Faial, here’s a sample itinerary that will help you make the most of your time on the island:

Day Activity
1 Explore Horta’s historic center and visit the Horta Regional Museum.
2 Take a boat tour to see whales and dolphins, and visit the Peter’s Cafe Sport.
3 Hike the Caldeira Loop Trail and enjoy a picnic lunch by the crater.
4 Take a scuba diving or snorkeling tour and enjoy the island’s beautiful underwater world.
5 Visit the Capelinhos Volcano Interpretation Centre and hike the Capelinhos Trail.
6 Take a culinary tour and sample Faial’s delicious seafood and wine.
7 Relax on one of Faial’s beautiful beaches and soak up the island’s natural beauty.

Food and Drink Scene of Faial

Faial is a foodie’s paradise, with a rich culinary tradition that is rooted in the island’s history and geography. Here are some of the must-try dishes and local beverages:

Must-Try Dishes

  • Caldeirada de Peixe: A hearty fish stew that is a staple of Faial’s cuisine.
  • Lapas: Grilled limpets that are a popular snack in Azores.
  • Espetadas: Skewered meat that is grilled over an open flame and served with potatoes and vegetables.
  • Queijadas: Sweet pastries that are made with cheese and sugar and are a must-try for dessert lovers.

Local Beverages

  • Verdelho: A crisp, aromatic white wine that is produced on the island and pairs perfectly with seafood.
  • Ginjinha: A sweet and tangy cherry liqueur that is a popular after-dinner drink.
  • Kima: A refreshing soda made with local passion fruit that is a perfect thirst-quencher on hot days.

Where to Stay in Faial: Hotels, Hostels, and Guesthouses

Faial offers a wide range of accommodations to suit every taste and budget, from luxury hotels and resorts to cozy guesthouses and hostels. Here are some of the best options:

  • Hotel do Canal: A luxurious hotel with a stunning ocean view, located in the heart of Horta.
  • Pousada Forte da Horta: A historic fortress turned into a charming hotel, located in the center of Horta.
  • Faial Resort Hotel: A modern hotel with a large swimming pool and beautiful gardens, located outside of Horta.
  • Angra Bed & Breakfast: A cozy and affordable guesthouse with a homey atmosphere, located in the picturesque village of Cedros.

What to Buy in Faial: Souvenirs and Local Products to Bring Home

Faial is known for its handicrafts, souvenirs, and local products that make for perfect gifts and mementos of your trip. Here are some of the best things to buy in Faial:

  • Scrimshaw: Intricately carved whale teeth and bones that are a testament to the island’s whaling tradition.
  • Azulejos: Blue-painted ceramic tiles that are a staple of Azorean architecture and make for beautiful wall decorations.
  • Local cheese: Faial is known for its delicious cheese, which comes in a variety of flavors and textures.
  • Wine and Liquors: Faial produces some of the best wines and liqueurs in the Azores, including the famous Verdelho wine and Ginjinha.

Local Culture and Customs of Faial

Faial has a vibrant culture that is deeply influenced by its history and geography. Some of the most important cultural events and customs include:

  • Festival of the Holy Spirit: A religious festival that takes place seven weeks after Easter and features parades, feasts, and other festivities.
  • Whaling: Although whaling is no longer practiced in Faial, the island’s whaling history is still celebrated through traditional songs, dances, and handicrafts.
  • Azorean music: Faial is home to many talented musicians who specialize in traditional Azorean music, which combines elements of Portuguese, African, and Celtic music.
  • Blue-painted houses: Faial’s distinctive blue-painted houses are a testament to the island’s maritime heritage and identity.

Best Time to Visit Faial

Faial is a year-round destination that offers something for everyone, depending on your preferences. The summer months (June to September) are the busiest and most popular time to visit, with warm weather, long days, and plenty of outdoor activities and events. The shoulder seasons (April-May and October-November) offer milder weather, fewer crowds, and more affordable prices, making them a great option for budget travelers. The winter months (December to March) are the quietest period, with a chance of rain and strong winds, but also the best time to see life-changing Atlantic swells hitting the Azorean coastline for some spectacular surfing.

Do’s and Don’ts of Faial

To ensure a smooth and enjoyable trip to Faial, here are some do’s and don’ts to keep in mind:

Do:

  • Try the local cuisine and sample the island’s wine and liquors.
  • Respect the island’s environment and natural beauty by avoiding littering or waste.
  • Hire a local guide to gain insights into Faial’s history and culture.
  • Bring comfortable walking shoes and outdoor gear as Faial is intended for a lot of outdoor activities.

Don’t:

  • Swim in unprotected areas due to the strong currents and tides in some parts of the island.
  • Disturb the local wildlife, including birds and marine life.
  • Rely on public transportation, renting a car or bicycle is advised for better touring.
  • Minimize the effect of the Covid-19 pandemic by taking precautions.

Safety and Health

Both safety and health are top priorities when it comes to traveling. Here is what you need to know about staying safe and healthy in Faial.

Safety in Faial

Faial is a safe place to travel as long as you take basic precautions such as avoiding deserted areas in the evening and taking care of your valuables. It’s better to avoid going swimming alone or in unprotected areas due to the strong currents and tides.

Health in Faial

Faial has a well-developed medical system, and you can find pharmacies and healthcare professionals in most towns and villages. Bring insect repellent and apply it liberally, especially if you are hiking in the forested areas of the island. Wear sunscreen, a hat, and light clothing to avoid sunburn in the island’s hot sun.

Getting to Faial: Transportation Options and Logistics

To get to Faial, you can take one of several transportation options, including:

  • Air travel: Faial Airport (HOR) is located near Horta and offers direct flights to other Azorean islands as well as Lisbon and Porto in mainland Portugal.
  • Sea travel: Ferry services operate between Faial and other Azorean islands, as well as mainland Portugal.

How to Get Around in Faial

The best way to get around in Faial is by car, bike, or walking. Renting a car is usually the most convenient option, as it allows you to explore the island at your own pace and reach more remote areas. There are also bike rental companies available for tourists. There is also a bus service that connects the island’s major towns and villages, but the frequency may not be as regular, so it is highly recommended to rent a vehicle.

Where to Go Next from Faial: Nearby Destinations and Excursions

If you have extra time on your trip to Faial and want to explore other nearby destinations, here are some ideas:

  • Pico Island: Known for its towering Pico Mountain and scenic vineyards, Pico is just a ferry ride away from Faial.
  • Sao Jorge Island: With its rugged coastline and traditional Azorean culture, Sao Jorge is another Azorean gem worth exploring.
  • Terceira Island: This island is known for its UNESCO World Heritage-listed city of Angra do Heroismo and its vibrant cultural scene.

Conclusion

Faial is a hidden gem in the Azores that is waiting to be discovered. Its stunning natural beauty, rich history, and vibrant culture make it an ideal destination for travelers who are looking for a unique and authentic experience. With this comprehensive guide as your companion, you are ready to plan your dream trip to Faial!
